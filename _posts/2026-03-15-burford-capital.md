Burford Capital (NYSE: BUR) has had a brutal twelve months. Shares have declined roughly 42%, recently trading near $7.92 — a level that implies the stock changes hands at approximately 0.75x tangible book value. For a company that has compounded its capital provision portfolio at a 26% IRR since inception, generated nearly $3.8 billion in cumulative realizations, and just posted 39% growth in Principal Finance segment commitments, that valuation gap demands explanation. Understanding what is actually happening — versus what the market seems to fear — is the key to sizing up what is genuinely a compelling risk-reward proposition.

---

## What the Market Is Worried About

The FY2025 earnings report, released February 26, 2026, did little to help sentiment. Total consolidated revenues fell 24% year-over-year to $413 million, and net income attributable to shareholders dropped 57% to $62.6 million. Q4 2025 was particularly ugly: quarterly revenue of just $33.4 million versus $93 million a year earlier, and a quarterly net loss attributable to shareholders of $37.5 million. The stock fell more than 11% in a single session.

Overlaid on weak reported results is a leadership transition. On March 3, 2026, Burford amended its employment agreement with President Aviva Will. Effective March 11, 2026, she assumed the role of Vice Chair and ceased to be an executive officer. Will was widely respected — Chambers and Partners called her "a titan in the industry" — and while the transition appears negotiated rather than abrupt, her shift to a non-executive role is a legitimate governance consideration.

And then there is Argentina.

---

## The YPF Overhang: Optionality, Not Liability

The YPF matter — Burford's financing of the Petersen and Eton Park claims against the Republic of Argentina arising from the 2012 nationalization of YPF S.A. — is simultaneously the company's most visible asset and its greatest source of volatility. In September 2023, the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York entered judgment in favor of the plaintiffs for approximately $16 billion, including roughly $8 billion in pre-judgment interest.

As of December 31, 2025, the combined fair value of YPF-related assets on a consolidated basis stood at **$2.6 billion**, with approximately $2.4 billion of that being unrealized gains atop a cost basis of just $193.6 million. On a Burford-only segment basis, the fair value was $1.7 billion. The concentration is significant: YPF-related assets accounted for approximately 46% of the consolidated fair value of capital provision assets at year-end.

The main appeal was argued before the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit on October 29, 2025. In a separate but related proceeding, the district court issued a turnover order requiring Argentina to transfer its YPF shares to an account in the United States; Argentina has appealed this order. A Second Circuit decision is expected in coming months, with further proceedings — including a potential Supreme Court petition — extending into 2027 under adverse scenarios.

Burford itself cautions that meaningful risks remain, including further proceedings in U.S. and foreign courts, appeals, enforcement actions, and collateral litigation — and that litigation matters often resolve for considerably less than the amount of any judgment.

Both cautions are fair. But they obscure something important about how to think about this asset in a valuation context. At $7.92 per share, with tangible book value of $10.57, the market is essentially saying the non-YPF business is worth less than its book value — a conclusion that seems difficult to justify given the operating fundamentals. To the extent the share price embeds little to no value for the YPF claim, the outcome of the appeal represents something closer to a free option than a priced-in asset.

## The Core Business: Better Than Headlines Suggest

Strip out the noise of lumpy realizations, fair value accounting, and Argentina, and Burford's underlying franchise is growing strongly.

**New Business Generation**

New definitive commitments in the Principal Finance segment rose 39% in 2025 to $871.7 million. On a group-wide basis, commitments reached $984.7 million, up 21% from the prior year. This is the pipeline for future cash realizations.

**Portfolio Scale**

The total group-wide portfolio — fair value of capital provision assets plus undrawn commitments — stood at $7.5 billion at year-end 2025, roughly steady year-over-year but with improved composition. The Burford-only Principal Finance portfolio grew 9% to $5.7 billion, and adjusted Burford-only deployments rose 15% to $459 million.

**Track Record**

Since inception, the concluded and partially concluded portfolio has generated an 83% cumulative ROIC and 26% IRR. The weighted average life of concluded assets sits at just 2.5 years (weighted by deployed cost) — meaning capital turns over rapidly by the standards of private asset classes.

**Why Did 2025 Earnings Disappoint?**

The earnings decline was primarily a timing story, not a quality story. Net realized gains in the Principal Finance segment fell 52% to $157.7 million. The company had two realized gains each exceeding $50 million in 2024; in 2025, none of that magnitude materialized. There was also a notable shift in expense recognition: case-related expenditures ineligible for inclusion in asset cost surged from $0.8 million to $14.6 million, largely due to the consolidation of the EP Funds (the Eton Park liquidation restructuring) and incremental legal costs Burford bore directly on certain matters. These do not represent a structural deterioration in underwriting discipline — they are quirks of consolidation accounting.

The operating cost base grew 17% in 2025, meaningful but still leaving the Burford-only operating margin robust. Finance costs rose 11% to $151 million as the 2033 Notes were layered into the stack — a deliberate balance sheet decision that bought duration at the cost of near-term earnings.

## Debt: Extended but Expensive

Burford's debt load warrants attention. Total debt at year-end 2025 stood at approximately $2.15 billion across five series of fixed-rate notes, against $6.6 billion in total assets and $3.1 billion in equity (including non-controlling interests), implying consolidated debt-to-equity of roughly 0.69x.

In January 2026, the company proactively addressed its only near-term maturity. On January 15, 2026, Burford completed a private offering of $500 million in 8.50% senior notes due 2034, with proceeds earmarked primarily to redeem the 5.000% bonds due 2026. The deal was upsized from an initially planned $450 million, with pricing completed the day after announcement — a pointed signal of institutional demand. The trade-off is obvious: swapping 5% money for 8.5% money is materially more expensive, pushing the pro forma weighted average cost of debt to 7.9% from 7.5%. However, the refinancing meaningfully extends Burford's maturity profile.

Post-refinancing, the pro forma weighted average debt maturity extends to 5.7 years, comfortably exceeding the 2.6-year weighted average life of the concluded portfolio — a structurally sound matching of asset and liability duration. The leverage covenants under the bond indentures (debt-to-equity thresholds ranging from 1.5x to 2.0x, depending on the series) are well within bounds, with reported ratios in the 0.82x–0.83x range.

## Valuation: Three Ways to Look at It

**1. Price-to-Tangible Book Value**

Tangible book value per share at year-end 2025 was $10.57. The stock at $7.92 implies approximately 0.75x P/TBV. Tangible book is itself a conservative figure: it is built on deployed costs and includes unrealized gains across the portfolio. Investors buying at $7.92 are paying roughly 75 cents on the dollar for assets that have generated an 83% ROIC over their history.

**2. Ex-YPF Residual Value**

If one ascribes zero value to YPF-related assets (Burford-only fair value of $1.7 billion), the remaining Burford-only portfolio is valued at approximately $2.2 billion ($3.9 billion minus $1.7 billion). Against a current market cap of approximately $1.73 billion, the core portfolio trades at roughly 0.79x its own fair value — excluding a claim against a sovereign backed by a $16 billion district court judgment, with enforcement proceedings underway in eight foreign jurisdictions.

**3. Sell-Side Coverage**

Both Deutsche Bank (target lowered to $15 from $16 post-results, Buy maintained) and Berenberg (Buy reiterated) remain constructive on the name. This is a stock where professional coverage suggests meaningful upside, though I would note that consensus target prices can vary widely depending on data source and whether one looks at the U.S. or London listing.

## The Management Alignment Signal

One development that rarely receives sufficient attention: on March 9, 2026, Burford disclosed that on March 5, executives invested over $4.3 million in ordinary shares through the company's deferred compensation plan, raising co-founders Christopher Bogart and Jonathan Molot's combined ownership to approximately 8.5%. This follows a recurring pattern of management converting personal cash compensation into equity, rather than relying solely on RSU vesting. With co-founder ownership at roughly $145 million at current prices — invested voluntarily, at a price they chose, after knowing the full FY2025 results — the alignment of interest is difficult to dismiss.

## Key Risks to the Thesis

This is not a simple buy. The bear case has genuine substance:

**YPF Enforcement Risk.** A Second Circuit reversal — or even a partial adverse ruling on damages methodology — would obliterate the bulk of Burford's unrealized fair value. Argentina has demonstrated a consistent pattern of delay, and recognition proceedings are currently pending in the United Kingdom, France, Ireland, Canada, Australia, Luxembourg, Brazil, and Cyprus, with Argentina contesting each proceeding vigorously. Even a favorable appellate ruling does not guarantee cash in hand.

**Earnings Lumpiness.** The model is irreducibly lumpy. Q4 2025 produced effectively zero segment-level operating income despite a healthy underlying portfolio. Burford-only cash receipts fell 24% in 2025 to $530 million, and investors must be comfortable with multi-quarter periods of apparent inactivity followed by sudden step-function realizations.

**Rising Financing Costs.** The blended cost of debt has risen meaningfully with the 8.5% 2034 notes. As long as portfolio ROIC stays around 83% and IRRs near 26%, the math works well. But if case duration extends — a real risk given court backlogs — levered return spread compression becomes a legitimate concern.

**Leadership Transition.** Aviva Will's shift to a non-executive role is a significant management change. She remains connected as Vice Chair under a two-year agreement, and the fundamentals of the investment team and Commitments Committee are intact. But her operational role overseeing origination and underwriting was substantial, and no successor as President has been publicly identified.

**Regulatory Pressure.** Litigation finance is attracting increasing regulatory attention globally. In the U.K., the government announced its intention to reverse the PACCAR ruling — a positive for the industry — but timing remains unclear. In the U.S., disclosure requirements are expanding incrementally at the federal court level through local rules and standing orders. None of these represent existential threats, but they add friction.

## The Investment Conclusion

Burford is not a simple stock. It is a levered, fair-value-accounted, litigation-exposed business with a binary $16 billion overhang and a management transition underway. That is precisely why it trades at 75 cents on the dollar relative to tangible book — the market is demanding a discount for complexity.

But complexity is not the same as impairment. The core franchise is growing at an accelerating pace. Principal Finance commitments are up 39%. The concluded portfolio track record remains intact at 83% ROIC and 26% IRR. The balance sheet has been proactively extended. And the people running the firm are investing millions of their own dollars in the stock at current prices.

For investors willing to underwrite three forms of uncertainty — YPF appellate risk, realization timing, and a management transition — Burford Capital offers what is genuinely rare in today's market: a fundamentals-driven business trading below the sum of its parts, with a near-term binary catalyst that the market appears to have largely written off. The upside case, if the Second Circuit affirms the district court and enforcement proceeds in even a fraction of the available jurisdictions, is measured in multiples of the current share price. The downside case, if YPF produces nothing but the non-YPF franchise continues on its current trajectory, is a stock that still looks cheap.

That is an asymmetry worth owning.
