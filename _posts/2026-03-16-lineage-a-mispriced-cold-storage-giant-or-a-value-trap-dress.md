## Structural Dominance at a Cyclical Low

Lineage, Inc. is the world's largest global temperature-controlled warehouse REIT — a designation confirmed in its SEC filings, not a marketing flourish. The company's network spans 501 warehouses across North America, Asia-Pacific, and Europe, comprising approximately 88 million square feet and 3.1 billion cubic feet of temperature-controlled capacity. Its top 25 customers use an average of 22 Lineage facilities each, and roughly 95% of global warehousing revenues come from countries where Lineage's local network is the largest by cubic feet. That kind of density creates pricing power, retention, and operational leverage that smaller competitors cannot replicate.

The stock has shed roughly 52% from its $78 IPO price and currently trades around $37. For a REIT of this scale, operating in an industry with high barriers to entry, the question is whether the market is pricing in a structural impairment or a cyclical trough — and that distinction is where the investment thesis lives or dies.

---

## The Core Problem: 9.5 Points of Excess Capacity

The industry's near-term pain is straightforward to diagnose. U.S. public refrigerated warehouse capacity expanded 14.5% between 2021 and 2025, while estimated consumer demand for the categories stored in Lineage's network grew only 5%, creating 9.5 percentage points of excess capacity. That surplus continues to pressure pricing and utilization across the sector, compounded by a post-pandemic inventory destocking cycle.

Management noted on its February 2026 earnings call that customer switching was most pronounced in the first one to two years after new supply came online, with some customers subsequently returning to Lineage because of service quality. Critically, the supply picture is already shifting: new capacity is expected to grow just 1.5% in 2026, a sharp deceleration. Customer inventories appear to be at trough levels. This is the single most important variable for the bull case.

## 2025 Financials: Ugly on the Surface, Better Underneath

The reported numbers invite dismissal. Lineage posted a GAAP net loss of $113 million in 2025 on approximately $5.36 billion of revenue. Same-store warehouse NOI fell 5.8% for the full year. The global warehousing segment margin contracted 190 basis points to 37.6%.

But the GAAP loss is largely noise for a REIT — depreciation, amortization, goodwill impairments, and IPO-related costs dominate. The cash earnings story is more constructive:

- Full-year AFFO increased 22.7% to $865 million; AFFO per share rose 2.4% to $3.37.
- Adjusted EBITDA of $1.298 billion declined only 2.3% year over year.
- Net cash from operations grew to $943 million from $703 million in 2024.

Most importantly, same-store physical occupancy improved sequentially by 400 basis points to 79.3% in Q4 2025, signaling a return to more normalized seasonality. That sequential improvement is the green shoot that separates this from a deteriorating story.

---

## 2026 Guidance: Honest, Not Optimistic

Management's 2026 outlook is refreshingly sober: same-store NOI growth of negative 4% to negative 1%, adjusted EBITDA of $1.25 to $1.30 billion, and AFFO per share of $2.75 to $3.00.

That AFFO guidance represents a year-over-year decline from the 2025 level of $3.37. The step-down is primarily a financing cost story, not an operating one — headwinds from expiring interest rate hedges and the annualized interest expense of bond issuances completed in 2025. Guided interest expense of $340 to $360 million is up sharply from $268 million in 2025.

The conservatism is deliberate. Management assumed a similar operating environment to 2025 and excluded potential upside from tariff resolution, lower interest rates, or tax relief. That builds in optionality. On the pricing front, Lineage expects a 1% to 2% net pricing increase in its warehousing segment and had already worked through 65% of its warehousing revenue base in contract negotiations by the time of the Q4 earnings call.

The company also identified a concrete self-help lever: line of sight to $50 million-plus of annualized cost savings by year-end 2026 from streamlining and centralizing select functions, with roughly half expected to benefit 2026 results. Management expects same-store NOI cadence to start at the lower end of the annual range and improve into the second half.

## The Technology Moat: Real, But With a Long Fuse

Lineage's competitive differentiation rests on pillars that competitors cannot easily replicate.

**Automation leadership.** Lineage operates 83 automated warehouses — 25 fully automated and 58 semi-automated. Company sustainability data indicates automated warehouses use approximately 20% less energy per pallet position than conventional warehouses in the same metropolitan areas. Lower operating costs and superior throughput compound over time.

**Proprietary software.** The LinOS warehouse execution system uses patented algorithms to optimize task allocation. LinOS is currently deployed in automated facilities and piloted in conventional warehouses. With global warehousing labor costs of approximately $1.5 billion in 2025, broad rollout could be a substantial margin catalyst — though rollout delays present near-term execution risk.

---

## Development Pipeline: Embedded Future Earnings

This is perhaps the most underappreciated aspect of the Lineage story. The company has 24 facilities under construction or in the process of ramping and stabilizing, which management projects will deliver over $150 million of incremental EBITDA once stabilized. Separately, the 10-K discloses a nine-project under-construction pipeline with an estimated total cost of $1.095 billion and $679 million of remaining spend, targeting a 10% weighted average NOI yield. Lineage has also invested $1.1 billion across 21 development projects expected to generate $185 million of annual NOI upon stabilization.

Combined with the $50 million of annualized cost savings, this development pipeline represents Lineage's internal path to a meaningful earnings recovery, independent of macro improvement.

## Valuation: Where the Debate Gets Interesting

At $37.25 per share with approximately 227 million shares outstanding, the market capitalization is roughly $8.5 billion. Net debt stands at $7.7 billion, yielding an enterprise value of approximately $16.2 billion.

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| EV / 2025 Adj. EBITDA ($1.298B) | ~12.5x |
| EV / 2026E Adj. EBITDA ($1.275B midpoint) | ~12.7x |
| 2026E AFFO yield ($2.875 midpoint / $37.25) | ~7.7% |
| Price / 2026E AFFO | ~13.0x |
| Dividend yield (annualized $2.11 / $37.25) | ~5.7% |

The EV/EBITDA multiples look inexpensive in absolute terms for a monopoly-scale infrastructure asset, but must be weighed against near-term NOI headwinds. The dividend — based on the most recently declared quarterly rate of $0.5275 per share, or $2.11 annualized — is covered by AFFO at the guidance midpoint, though with limited headroom.

Management has specifically flagged that it will explore opportunities to address the valuation mismatch between the public and private markets, including joint ventures and partial monetization actions that could surface asset values meaningfully above the current stock price.

## Three Material Overhangs That Deserve Full Credit

**1. Leverage.** Net debt ended 2025 at $7.7 billion; net debt to adjusted EBITDA stood at 6.0x, with an adjusted net debt to transaction-adjusted EBITDA ratio of 5.2x after accounting for development pipeline capital. Guided interest expense of $340 to $360 million for 2026 — versus $268 million in 2025 — underscores the cost of elevated leverage in a higher-for-longer rate environment.

**2. Securities class action.** The lawsuit *City of St. Clair Shores Police and Fire Retirement System v. Lineage, Inc.* (No. 25-cv-12383, E.D. Mich.) charges Lineage and certain executives, directors, IPO underwriters, and its IPO sponsor with violations of the Securities Act of 1933. The complaint alleges materially false or misleading statements, including that Lineage was experiencing sustained weakening in customer demand at the time of the IPO, that pre-IPO price increases were unsustainable, and that competitive advantages could not offset the adverse demand environment. These are allegations, not adjudicated facts — but the litigation adds headline risk and management distraction, with outcome and magnitude genuinely uncertain.

**3. Internal controls material weakness.** As confirmed by PricewaterhouseCoopers in the 2025 audit opinion, Lineage has a material weakness in information technology general controls — specifically in user access and program change management. While the auditors still issued a clean opinion on the financial statements themselves, a material weakness represents operational risk in a company managing a complex, technology-reliant global network, and it is a governance red flag for institutional investors with mandates that screen on these criteria.

## Competitive Context: Lineage vs. Americold

Americold Realty Trust is the other major publicly traded cold-storage REIT, though meaningfully smaller. Lineage's 3.1 billion cubic feet of capacity compares with Americold's approximately 1.4 billion. Lineage's automation footprint of 83 facilities dwarfs Americold's. On leverage, Americold's net debt-to-EBITDA ratio stood at approximately 6.8x at the end of 2025, above Lineage's 6.0x. Both companies are navigating the same industry oversupply — the differentiation lies in technology capability, network density, and the speed at which operational self-help initiatives can offset top-line pressure.

## Who Should Own This, and Under What Conditions

LINE is appropriate for **patient value investors** who can hold through an oversupply trough and are comfortable with AFFO declining in 2026 before potentially recovering as the development pipeline ramps and new supply additions slow materially. It suits **income-oriented investors** willing to accept elevated leverage risk alongside a ~5.7% dividend yield. And it may interest **special situation investors** monitoring management's stated intention to pursue joint ventures or partial asset monetizations — transactions that could surface private market valuations well above the current public price.

LINE is inappropriate for investors with short time horizons, those with internal controls mandates, or leveraged and concentrated portfolios exposed to the litigation overhang.

---

## Bottom Line: A Trough Pricing Opportunity With Eyes Open

Lineage sits at the intersection of three durable structural themes — global food supply chain infrastructure, warehouse automation, and cold chain logistics technology — and trades at trough valuations because of a cyclical capacity glut that is now measurably improving, a complex IPO transition, and governance issues that a newly public company is still working through.

The key catalysts for re-rating are identifiable: new supply growth falling to 1.5% in 2026, $185 million of development NOI upon stabilization beginning to flow through, $50 million-plus of cost savings ramping, and potential asset monetization actions that close the private-public valuation gap. At roughly 13x forward AFFO with a 7.7% AFFO yield, the stock does not require perfection — it requires patience.

The bear case is straightforward: 6x leverage in a soft demand environment with rising interest costs, a pending securities class action, and a material controls weakness. Neither the bull nor the bear case should be dismissed. Position sizing accordingly.
