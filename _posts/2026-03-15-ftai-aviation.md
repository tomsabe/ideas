FTAI Aviation has compounded at a pace that would embarrass most private equity funds. A $100 investment made at year-end 2020 was worth over $1,188 by the end of 2025 — a return that dwarfs the S&P Midcap 400's $155 and the Dow Jones US Aerospace index's $245 over the same period. Yet the stock currently trades at $222, down roughly 31% from its 52-week high near $323. The pullback creates a real question: is the market repricing a structurally superior business, or fairly discounting an increasingly complex and execution-dependent story?

The answer, I believe, is both — and the difference between "buy the dip" and "wait for clarity" hinges on how seriously investors take three simultaneous strategic pivots occurring on top of an already high-velocity base business.

---

## The Core MRE Business: Still the Crown Jewel

Before addressing the new bets, investors should understand what they own. FTAI is a leading independent maintenance, repair, and exchange (MRE) platform for the CFM56-5B, CFM56-7B, and V2500 engines — the narrow-body workhorses that power the Boeing 737NG and Airbus A320ceo fleets. Thousands of these aircraft remain in service globally, most with meaningful remaining economic life.

The MRE model is elegantly simple: airlines historically send their engines to an OEM-affiliated shop for a full overhaul (expensive, slow, with the aircraft grounded for weeks). FTAI offers an exchange instead — swap an unserviceable engine for a rebuilt serviceable one from FTAI's pool, pay a fixed fee, and return to flying within days. For the airline, it is cheaper and faster. For FTAI, it creates recurring, volume-driven revenue with structural cost advantages as its parts pool deepens.

Full-year 2025 Aerospace Products Adjusted EBITDA reached $671.3 million — up 76% versus 2024 and over four times 2023's result. That is not the growth rate of a maturing business. It reflects both genuine demand and a capacity ramp that is still early-stage. FTAI exceeded its 2025 module production target, delivering 757 CFM56 modules versus a goal of 750, with fourth-quarter production reaching 228 modules — demonstrating sequential acceleration throughout the year.

The structural tailwinds are real. New engine deliveries (LEAP-1A, LEAP-1B, GTF) remain years behind schedule, meaning airlines are extending the service lives of their current fleets. Historically low aircraft retirement rates and a shift toward heavier maintenance overhauls signal a longer economic useful life for CFM56 and V2500 platforms. This dynamic directly benefits FTAI's addressable market — more engines in service, longer, requiring more MRE cycles.

Margin expansion is now management's stated focus. CEO Joseph Adams has said the company aims to grow aerospace products margins from 35% to 40% by leveraging PMA high-pressure turbine blades, lower-cost parts through its CFM materials agreement, and expanded piece-part repair capabilities — with the 40% target set for 2026.

## Three Big Bets That Define the Debate

The central investment question is not about the core MRE business — that case is well-established. The debate is about whether management is compounding its advantage into three adjacent platforms, or overextending.

### Strategic Capital Initiative: From Asset Owner to Asset Manager

The SCI is perhaps FTAI's most structurally important transformation. Rather than owning on-lease aircraft on its own balance sheet (capital-intensive, leverage-heavy), FTAI now manages third-party institutional capital to buy those assets, retaining a minority stake and earning management and servicing fees — plus profit participation.

FTAI raised $2 billion in equity commitments for SCI-1, which management describes as the largest fund ever dedicated to narrow-body mid-life aircraft. With leverage, 276 aircraft have been closed under letter of intent, representing $5.3 billion of the $6 billion target, with full deployment anticipated by end of Q2 2026.

The strategic logic is compelling. SCI-1 simultaneously: (1) offloads balance sheet assets at gain-on-sale, (2) locks in a captive pipeline of engines that will require MRE services for the life of the fund, and (3) generates recurring fee income. It transforms an asset-heavy leasing business into a fee-generative asset management platform — a re-rating event if sustained. Fundraising for SCI-2 has already begun, with an anchor equity commitment in place.

The risk: SCI-2 deployment competes for capital and management bandwidth, and FTAI carries execution risk as servicer of both partnerships. Returns to institutional LPs must be sustained for the fee model to remain credible over time.

### FTAI Power: Jet Engines as Data Center Turbines

The most speculative — and most discussed — new initiative. FTAI Power is a platform focused on converting CFM56 engines into aeroderivative gas turbines for data center and distributed power applications, with first production units expected in Q4 2026.

The engineering logic is sound: the process involves reconfiguring the core of a CFM56 engine into a compact power plant by replacing the aviation fuel system to burn natural gas and swapping the large front fan for hardware to drive an electrical generator. The result is a 25-megawatt unit that can start quickly, cycle flexibly, and fit on a small pad footprint. With over 22,000 CFM56 engines produced, this turbine offers a more scaled alternative to a market facing multi-year backlogs for power solutions.

The speed-to-power angle deserves particular attention. Trailer-mounted units are designed to be deployed on-site in approximately two weeks. For hyperscalers facing multi-year grid interconnection queues, a deployable 25MW unit is an attractive bridge solution.

The first aeroderivative product — FTAI Mod-1 — is expected to be delivered in Q4 2026, with planned production of 100 units in 2027. Management has already committed capital: inventory increased by approximately $150 million in Q4 2025 to secure additional turbines for the 2026 ramp, and the company began retrofitting its Montreal facility to establish a dedicated power production line — kept separate from aerospace operations for regulatory and asset integrity reasons.

Skeptics will note this is pre-revenue, pre-delivery, and requires FTAI to execute in an industry it has not operated in before. Power is a longer-dated call option — potentially very valuable, but not yet in the earnings model.

### OEM Partnership and PMA Parts: Deepening the Moat

The least-discussed but arguably most durable value driver is FTAI's deepening parts supply chain. The company entered a multiyear materials agreement with CFM, ensuring OEM part supply resilience and supporting the scaling of the module remanufacturing platform. Additionally, FTAI is pursuing PMA (Parts Manufacturer Approval) high-pressure turbine blades — proprietary replacement parts manufactured to FAA standards outside the OEM channel. Management has also cited AI-driven operational optimizations via a Palantir partnership as a driver of its targeted 39% production increase in 2026.

These are not flashy headlines, but they represent structural cost and supply advantages that competitors cannot easily replicate. Incremental savings per module on a 1,000-plus module annual run rate compound into material EBITDA expansion over time.

## The Elephant in the Room: Accounting Integrity

No honest FTAI analysis can sidestep January 2025. FTAI Aviation's stock fell 24%, from $153.29 to $116.08 per share on January 15, 2025, following the publication of a Muddy Waters report alleging the company had been recording one-time engine sales as recurring MRO revenue. A second report from Snowcap subsequently reinforced some of those claims.

FTAI's Audit Committee, after a review by independent legal and forensic accounting experts, concluded the allegations were unsupported and without merit. The company filed its 2025 10-K on time. The financials were audited by KPMG (following a change from Ernst & Young), and KPMG issued an unqualified opinion.

However, investors should still engage with the substance of the concerns rather than dismissing them entirely. The short sellers' central claim — that FTAI's Aerospace segment Adjusted EBITDA overstates recurring cash earnings by classifying volatile engine sales as MRE revenue — remains a legitimate analytical question even if fraud allegations were dismissed. The 10-K shows operating cash flow was negative $311 million in 2025, driven heavily by inventory build. Free cash flow generation — while guided at approximately $915 million for 2026 — is deeply dependent on the pace of asset cycling, SCI sales, and insurance recoveries.

The 2025 annual report disclosed that $54.3 million of that year's Aviation Leasing EBITDA came from Russian insurance claim settlements — clearly non-recurring. The $46.4 million gain on sale to the 2025 Partnership is also one-time in nature. Stripping these out, the underlying earnings quality of the Leasing segment is more modest than headline EBITDA implies.

Serious investors should build both a "management's narrative" model and a "recurring cash earnings" model, and invest only if the gap between the two is adequately covered by the valuation.

## 2026 Guidance and What the Numbers Imply

Management raised 2026 total segment EBITDA guidance to $1.625 billion — $1.05 billion from Aerospace Products and $575 million from Aviation Leasing. Free cash flow is expected at approximately $915 million after increased investments in SCI-2 and FTAI Power.

At today's price of $222 and approximately 102.6 million shares outstanding, FTAI's market capitalization is roughly $22.8 billion. Including net debt of approximately $3.15 billion, enterprise value is approximately $26 billion.

On 2026 guided EBITDA of $1.625 billion, that implies an EV/EBITDA multiple of approximately 16x. On 2026 analyst consensus revenue estimates of roughly $3.3 billion, the stock trades at approximately 7.9x EV/sales. Neither multiple is cheap in absolute terms, but the context matters:

- **The MRE business has characteristics of a high-quality industrial compounder:** recurring revenue, structural cost advantages, pricing power rooted in time criticality for airlines.
- **The SCI model, if it scales into SCI-2 and beyond, could justify asset-management-style multiples** on fee income streams.
- **The Power business is currently unmonetized**, meaning investors are not yet paying for it — but the inventory build and facility retrofit signal management conviction.

Analyst consensus targets range from $225 to $350. FTAI's $1.625 billion EBITDA guidance sits above the current consensus estimate of roughly $1.49 billion. The stock is effectively back to where it was before the Power announcement surge, which looks like a reasonable reset point.

## Key Risks

**Execution risk on three simultaneous pivots.** SCI-2 fundraising, Power commercialization, and margin expansion in MRE are all competing for the same management bandwidth and balance sheet resources in 2026.

**CFM56 obsolescence tail.** The engine fleet that powers FTAI's franchise is aging. A sudden spike in fuel prices or easing of supply chain constraints could accelerate retirements and dent demand. FTAI will eventually need to prove it can manage the transition to supporting newer engine types.

**Leverage.** With $3.5 billion of unsecured senior notes and no debt maturities until 2028, the near-term structure is manageable. But the company operates with meaningful financial leverage at 2.6x net debt/EBITDA. FTAI has achieved strong BB-category credit ratings across all three major agencies. A further upgrade could reduce financing costs, but cyclical stress would amplify downside.

**Litigation overhang.** Securities class action lawsuits related to the short-seller episode remain pending in the Southern District of New York. While the Audit Committee concluded the allegations were without merit, legal expenses and management distraction are real costs with uncertain duration.

**Negative operating cash flow.** The company burned $311 million in operating cash in 2025 — the second consecutive year of negative operational cash generation. This is largely explained by inventory accumulation in support of MRE growth and Power feedstock, but it means FTAI is dependent on asset sales and debt markets for liquidity.

## Who Should Own This, and At What Price

FTAI is not a simple, low-risk compounder. It is a high-conviction, execution-dependent business undergoing rapid strategic transformation, trading at a premium multiple with multiple sources of uncertainty simultaneously live.

For investors who believe: (1) CFM56 demand remains elevated through at least 2028–2030, (2) the SCI model scales into a durable, fee-generating asset management franchise, and (3) FTAI Power delivers its first units on schedule in Q4 2026 — the current price near $222 represents a plausible entry point. The EBITDA guidance raise and continued module production growth are substantive positives that the market appears to be discounting alongside near-term cash flow concerns.

For investors who require cleaner free cash flow visibility, lower execution risk, or a more conservative balance sheet, the entry point is probably somewhere below $200 — or after the first FTAI Power unit ships and is recognized as revenue, providing proof of concept.

The investment case is real. But it demands active monitoring, not passive holding. The next twelve months will determine whether three simultaneous strategic bets look brilliant in retrospect, or whether management is stretched too thin to execute on all fronts simultaneously.
