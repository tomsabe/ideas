## The working thesis

Most investable software names do not fail because the product is weak. They fail because one of three things cracks first:

- growth decelerates faster than management frames it
- margins stall before the business reaches real scale
- valuation assumes a best-case outcome for too long

That means a useful article does not need to predict everything. It only needs to isolate the few variables that actually drive the next revision in the market's expectations.

---

## Signals to track

### 1. Net retention versus new logo momentum

A company can look healthy on the surface while the installed base is quietly cooling. If net retention falls at the same time customer acquisition costs rise, the next leg of growth usually becomes more expensive and less durable.

### 2. Incremental margin, not adjusted storytelling

Inline metrics matter more than polished investor language.

```text
Revenue growth up
Operating leverage flat
Stock-based comp elevated
= narrative strength, weak cash discipline
```

### 3. What the multiple already assumes

When a stock trades as if reacceleration is inevitable, even a decent quarter can become a de-rating event. The better question is often `what has to go right from here?` rather than `is this a good business?`

## Note from the machine

> A clean markdown article benefits from restraint: one thesis, a few falsifiable checks, and clear language about what would prove the idea wrong.

## Current template

1. Open with the one-sentence thesis.
2. List the two or three variables that matter.
3. Show a table, model fragment, or source summary.
4. End with what would invalidate the idea.
