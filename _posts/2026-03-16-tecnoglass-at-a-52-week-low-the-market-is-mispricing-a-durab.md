## A Quality Business at a Distressed Price

Tecnoglass Inc. (TGLS) is down approximately 38% over the past year, recently trading near its 52-week low of $40.09 against a 52-week high of $90.34. The culprit is straightforward: a toxic combination of near-record aluminum costs, tariff headwinds, and a strengthening Colombian peso that compressed margins faster than management anticipated through the back half of 2025.

But the market has conflated a cyclical cost problem with structural business deterioration. These are not the same thing. The backlog is at a record high, residential order momentum is accelerating, a meaningful new product category is inflecting, and management is aggressively buying back stock near multi-year lows. The current price represents a compelling entry point for investors with an 18–36 month horizon.

---

## The Structural Moat in Plain Sight

Tecnoglass is a vertically integrated manufacturer of high-specification architectural glass, aluminum windows, and vinyl windows, headquartered in Miami and manufacturing primarily out of Barranquilla, Colombia. The company serves approximately 1,000 customers across the Americas, with the United States accounting for roughly 95% of total revenues. It ranks among the largest glass fabricators serving the U.S. market and is the leading architectural glass transformation company in Colombia.

The competitive moat rests on three interlocking pillars that competitors cannot easily replicate:

**Vertical integration.** Tecnoglass controls the full value chain — from raw glass transformation through aluminum extrusion, window assembly, and U.S. distribution and installation. This eliminates middlemen, preserves margin at each step, and enables tight quality control. A joint venture with Saint-Gobain provides stable glass supply and accounts for approximately 60% of glass purchases.

**A structural labor cost advantage.** Manufacturing in Barranquilla means labor costs running approximately 7x below U.S. equivalents — roughly $3.50 per hour in Colombia versus $24 in the U.S. This isn't about cutting corners; it's about geography. The company pays above local minimum wage and invests heavily in its workforce.

**Logistics economics that defy intuition.** The Barranquilla facility sits near three major Colombian ports. Because of the structural U.S.–Colombia shipping imbalance (containers return south semi-empty), Tecnoglass often secures maritime freight at rates below what domestic U.S. manufacturers pay for ground transport. Intercompany transport costs run below 5% of revenues.

These advantages produce a financial profile that is genuinely extraordinary for a building products company. Tecnoglass's three-year average return on equity of 38% and return on invested capital of 27% dwarf peer averages of 14% and 9%, respectively.

## What Went Wrong: Dissecting the Margin Compression

The bull case doesn't require pretending 2025 was pristine. It wasn't. Four headwinds hit simultaneously:

**Aluminum costs spiked.** Gross margin fell to 40.0% in Q4 2025 from 44.5% in Q4 2024, driven primarily by sharply higher aluminum costs, tariffs, and currency effects. Aluminum prices surged to near all-time highs in the quarter.

**Tariff burden.** Full-year selling, general, and administrative expense climbed to 20% of revenue from 17.2% in the prior year, driven substantially by tariffs absorbed during 2025 alongside higher transportation and salary expenses.

**Peso appreciation.** The Colombian peso strengthened approximately 9.5% year-over-year in Q4 and approximately 12% for the full year, moving from roughly 4,308 to 3,791 per U.S. dollar. With approximately 20–25% of costs denominated in pesos while nearly all revenue is in dollars, this was a direct hit to margins with no revenue offset.

**Revenue mix shift.** A higher proportion of installation revenue (lower margin than product-only sales) weighed on consolidated gross margins, particularly in Q4.

Critically, these headwinds are either cyclical (aluminum prices, currency) or addressable (tariff mitigation, pricing actions). The underlying demand engine — evidenced by a record backlog — was unimpaired throughout.

## Three Catalysts the Market Is Underweighting

### The Backlog Provides Multi-Year Earnings Visibility

Backlog increased 16.1% to a record $1.3 billion. Residential orders grew 20% year-over-year in Q4, with strength continuing into early 2026. Multi-family and commercial markets produced another quarter of record backlog with a project pipeline extending well into 2027.

The book-to-bill ratio has been above 1.1x for 20 consecutive quarters — meaning new orders consistently exceed revenue recognized. The project cancellation rate is near zero. Remaining performance obligations at year-end stood at $912.2 million, with $492.9 million expected to convert to revenue in 2026 alone. This is not a "hope" business. Revenue visibility is unusually high.

### Vinyl Is an Underappreciated Step-Change in Addressable Market

Tecnoglass entered the vinyl window market in late 2023, a move management estimates more than doubles its addressable market. The vinyl ramp was slower than initially projected — management acknowledged this frankly on the Q4 call — but the trajectory is clear. Vinyl deliveries ramped meaningfully in the second half of 2025, and management expects further acceleration in 2026 as dealer additions increase and certifications expand geographically.

Vinyl is also structurally important: it carries less aluminum exposure, which reduces the company's commodity risk over time. As vinyl scales as a proportion of total revenue, earnings quality improves — less volatile, less tariff-exposed.

### Structural Americanization: Redomiciliation + Potential U.S. Facility

Two strategic announcements from the February earnings release deserve more attention than they received:

**Redomiciliation from the Cayman Islands to the U.S.** The board approved a plan, subject to shareholder vote, to simplify the company's organizational and regulatory structure, improve the tax efficiency of dividend distributions, and — perhaps most importantly — broaden the potential investor base. Many institutional mandates explicitly exclude Cayman-domiciled companies. The shareholder vote is expected within months.

**Feasibility study for a new U.S. manufacturing facility.** The proposed plant would be highly automated, designed to diversify the operational footprint, improve lead times for certain markets, enhance supply chain efficiencies, and open access to Buy America–qualified projects — a significant market currently inaccessible. If the company moves forward, 2026 investment would be limited to an estimated $20–$25 million for land acquisition only. This is optionality at modest cost.

## Geographic Expansion: The Florida Moat Extends Nationally

Florida still represents over 90% of U.S. revenue, but diversification is advancing. Showrooms are operational in New York, Charleston, Houston, and Bonita Springs, with Phoenix and Los Angeles in lease negotiations. Nearly 13.1% of the U.S. backlog is now for projects outside Florida.

The commercial backlog is increasingly diversified — the Northeast and Texas are generating meaningful new project wins. The showroom strategy is a low-capital, high-leverage model: each showroom generates leads for both the legacy aluminum product line and the new vinyl offering, physically establishing the brand in new geographies.

## 2026 Financial Outlook: The Recovery Setup

For 2026, Tecnoglass guided revenue of $1.06 billion to $1.13 billion, representing approximately 11% growth at the midpoint. Adjusted EBITDA guidance is $265 million to $305 million, incorporating elevated aluminum costs, labor dynamics, and foreign exchange assumptions into its baseline.

The guidance range reflects two meaningfully different macro scenarios:

- **High end ($1.13B / $305M EBITDA):** Fed rate cuts support mortgage affordability; aluminum prices soften roughly 10% from year-end 2025 levels; Colombian peso depreciates toward COP 4,000/USD; full backlog execution without delays.

- **Low end ($1.06B / $265M EBITDA):** No Fed cuts; stable but elevated aluminum; peso remains below COP 3,800/USD, keeping costs elevated.

Management indicated Q1 2026 will be softer, with invoicing expected to improve in the second quarter and beyond as orders convert. Patient investors should not be surprised by a weak first-quarter print in late April.

## Valuation: Compelling at Current Prices

At approximately $43.86 per share, TGLS trades at:

- **~12.5x trailing earnings** (FY2025 EPS of $3.42)
- **~7.7x trailing EV/Adjusted EBITDA** ($291M FY2025 adjusted EBITDA; ~$2.1B enterprise value)
- **~2.1x trailing revenues** ($984M FY2025)

Consensus estimates place 2026 EPS at approximately $3.61, implying roughly 12x forward earnings. That is an unusual discount for a company that:

- Has compounded revenue at a mid-to-high teens CAGR over the past decade
- Generates three-year average ROE of 38% and ROIC of 27%
- Holds a record backlog with near-zero cancellation rates
- Carries net leverage of just 0.24x

The majority of analysts covering the stock rate it a Buy, with a consensus price target near $68 — implying roughly 55% upside. Even the most conservative target implies substantial appreciation from current levels.

For a simple DCF framing: assume $285 million EBITDA in 2026 (midpoint guidance), $320 million in 2027, and $360 million in 2028 (consistent with analyst estimates and historical growth rates), apply a 12x EBITDA exit multiple (conservative for this quality profile), and discount back at 10%. The fair value lands in the mid-$60s — consistent with sell-side consensus.

**The bear case** is not zero — it's a scenario where aluminum and the Colombian peso remain permanently elevated AND vinyl fails to scale AND the backlog executes slowly. That combination is possible but would require multiple adverse factors to persist simultaneously over several years.

## Capital Allocation: Management Is Voting With Its Checkbook

Tecnoglass repurchased $118 million in shares in 2025 — approximately 5% of beginning-of-year shares outstanding — and expanded its aggregate repurchase authorization to $250 million. After cumulative repurchases of roughly $140 million since inception, about $110 million remains.

At current prices, $110 million would retire roughly 5% of total shares outstanding. This is aggressive, meaningful capital return for a mid-cap compounder.

The company generated $135.8 million in operating cash flow and ended 2025 with approximately $465 million in liquidity and net leverage of just 0.24x. There is no financial stress here. The buyback is not a distress signal — it is a management team signaling that at $44 per share, the stock is obviously cheap relative to intrinsic value.

## Risks: Know What You Own

**Aluminum prices and tariffs.** This is the central near-term risk. Aluminum costs are structurally elevated relative to 2023–2024 levels, and the current tariff regime on aluminum extrusion is a real operating cost. Management has partially mitigated this by sourcing more U.S.-cast aluminum and states it has measures in place to offset 50% U.S. aluminum tariffs, including alternative raw material sources and favorable pricing arrangements with customers. But further tariff escalation remains a risk.

**Colombian peso.** Further appreciation of the peso versus the dollar would increase cost of goods sold. The peso appreciated 12% in 2025 — that was exceptional. Normalization to more historically typical levels would represent a meaningful tailwind, but there is no guarantee of such a move.

**Execution risk on new initiatives.** Vinyl scale-up, showroom-driven geographic expansion, and a potential U.S. facility all carry execution risk. None of these are certain to succeed on management's stated timeline.

**Concentrated ownership and governance.** Energy Holding Corporation, controlled by the Daes family, owns approximately 43% of shares outstanding. The planned redomiciliation from the Cayman Islands to the U.S. would improve the governance framework, but concentrated family ownership is a permanent feature.

**Single facility concentration.** The majority of production comes from a single complex in Barranquilla. A natural disaster, labor disruption, or infrastructure failure at that facility would be materially disruptive.

## A Compounder Mispriced on Cyclical Noise

Tecnoglass is not a broken business. It is a high-quality, vertically integrated manufacturer experiencing a well-defined cyclical margin trough driven by three temporary factors — an aluminum price spike, tariff burden, and peso appreciation — that are already partially reversing.

The record backlog, accelerating residential orders, vinyl product inflection, aggressive buyback program, and nascent geographic diversification all point to a business that is structurally stronger entering 2026 than it was entering 2025. Yet the stock is priced as though the margin compression is permanent.

At approximately 12x forward earnings, 7.7x trailing EBITDA, with $110 million of remaining buyback capacity and $465 million of total liquidity against net debt of only $71 million, the downside appears well-bounded. The upside — a return to low-40s gross margins as aluminum normalizes and the peso depreciates modestly — would translate to earnings power above current consensus and a re-rating toward the mid-teens P/E that this quality of business typically commands.

For value-oriented investors willing to look through near-term margin noise at a durable competitive moat, Tecnoglass at these levels is the kind of setup that rarely presents itself.
