## Investment Thesis

Hinge Health (NYSE: HNGE) is the dominant pure-play in digital musculoskeletal (MSK) care — a market that costs the U.S. healthcare system more annually than cardiovascular disease, cancer, or diabetes individually. The stock has pulled back roughly 34% from its post-IPO high near $62, creating an entry point that does not adequately reflect: (1) the compounding nature of its proprietary data and AI advantages, (2) the structural shift from a high-loss pre-IPO entity to a genuine cash machine, and (3) a TAM expansion story — via HingeSelect and Medicare — that the market is barely pricing. For growth-oriented investors with a 2–3 year horizon, this pullback looks like an opportunity.

## The Financial Inflection No One Is Talking About

Forget the headline GAAP losses for a moment. The most underappreciated aspect of this story is how rapidly unit economics have snapped into place.

In FY 2023, Hinge was burning cash at roughly a (23%) free cash flow margin. By FY 2025, it generated $180 million in free cash flow on $588 million in revenue — a 31% FCF margin. That is not a modest improvement; it is a fundamental business model validation.

| Metric | FY 2025 | FY 2024 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $588M | $390M | +51% |
| Non-GAAP Gross Margin | 83% | 78% | +500 bps |
| Non-GAAP Operating Income | $119M | $(26M) | Swing of $145M |
| Non-GAAP Operating Margin | 20% | (7%) | +2,700 bps |
| Free Cash Flow | $180M | $45M | +297% |
| FCF Margin | 31% | 12% | +1,900 bps |
| Rule of 40 Score | 81 | 45 | +36 pts |

The gross margin trajectory deserves special attention. Non-GAAP gross margin expanded from 70% in 2023 to 83% in 2025 — roughly 1,300 basis points in two years. The driver is structural: replacing physical sensors and tablet kits with TrueMotion's smartphone-based AI motion tracking eliminated major hardware costs, while generative AI tooling has dramatically reduced care team hours. According to the company's 10-K, the platform now requires approximately 97% fewer human care-team hours compared to traditional physical therapy — a figure that compounds as the member base grows.

Q4 2025 was particularly strong: revenue of $171 million (46% year-over-year growth), non-GAAP EPS of $0.49, non-GAAP gross margin of 85%, and non-GAAP operating margin of 28%.

## The GAAP Confusion: Why the Headline Loss Is Misleading

GAAP losses in 2025 were enormous — a net loss of $528 million and a GAAP operating loss of $546 million on $588 million in revenue. This has understandably confused some investors. The explanation is straightforward and non-recurring.

The May 2025 IPO triggered the vesting of pre-IPO RSUs and PRSUs that had been accumulating service credit for years. When the liquidity event condition was satisfied, Hinge recognized $643 million in stock-based compensation expense in a single year. Of that, $571 million was recognized at the IPO registration statement's effectiveness — a one-time accounting charge with no cash impact. The underlying cash business, as confirmed by $180 million in actual free cash flow, is unambiguously healthy.

Going forward, quarterly SBC should normalize substantially. Q4 2025 stock-based compensation was approximately $17 million, suggesting the GAAP/non-GAAP gap will compress materially.

## The Moat: Data Network Effects and Deep Switching Costs

The investment case rests partly on a moat argument the market has not fully priced. Hinge has accumulated a dataset from over 1.5 million lifetime members, approximately 106 million activity sessions, and more than 39 million member-reported outcome logs. Every session makes the AI model smarter — better at predicting which members are high-risk, which exercise progressions improve adherence, and which interventions prevent surgery. This is a genuine data network effect: scale begets accuracy, accuracy begets outcomes, and outcomes beget client retention.

The retention data is telling: 97% annual client retention, 100% historical partner retention, and net dollar retention above 110%. These are SaaS-comparable metrics in a healthcare context. Three of the company's largest revenue contributors — HCSC (17.5% of revenue), Elevance (13.7%), and Aetna (10.5%) — are also its distribution partners, meaning their contracts are deeply embedded in infrastructure. Dislodging Hinge from a large self-insured employer is operationally expensive for all parties.

The patent estate reinforces this: 40 issued patents and 88 pending applications for TrueMotion alone, plus 8 issued and 12 pending for Enso, with U.S. patents beginning to expire no earlier than July 2033.

## Competitive Landscape: Consolidation Creates Distance

Sword Health's January 2026 announcement that it would acquire rival Kaia Health for $285 million is a telling signal. That consolidation dynamic actually strengthens Hinge's position. Sword acquiring Kaia creates integration risk, management distraction, and a combined entity that has not yet proved public-market discipline. Hinge, meanwhile, cited winning a large enterprise with over 200,000 lives that left a competitor in the final month of 2025, contributing to an all-time high competitive win rate exiting the year.

Omada Health (OMDH) is a relevant public comparable. Its platform is broader — spanning diabetes, hypertension, and MSK — but Hinge leads on revenue scale ($588 million versus Omada's $260 million), gross margin (83% non-GAAP versus Omada's upper-60s% range), and FCF generation. Focused pure-plays with demonstrated margin profiles generally earn premium multiples over diversified platforms with lower unit economics.

## Three Growth Vectors the Market Underprices

The market is primarily pricing Hinge as a self-insured employer business. That is reasonable — it represents approximately 22 million of the company's roughly 24.6 million contracted lives. But three growth vectors are underappreciated:

**1. Annual Yield Expansion**

Hinge's annual yield — the percentage of eligible lives who become active paying members — was 3.9% in FY 2025, up 50 basis points year-over-year. This number has a long runway. Hinge has approximately 20 million average eligible lives but only roughly 783,000 members. Even moving yield from 3.9% to 5% on the existing base would generate an estimated $120–140 million in incremental revenue, with near-100% incremental gross margins. This is the highest-ROI growth driver in the business.

**2. HingeSelect**

Announced in June 2025, HingeSelect is a high-performance in-person provider network for MSK care — delivering spending up to 30–50% below commercial benchmarks, with triage by Hinge's in-house orthopedic physicians. This is a meaningful TAM expansion: rather than capturing only the digital therapy spend, Hinge can now capture downstream procedural spend. Early availability covers several hundred thousand eligible lives. Management is actively upselling existing clients, and the margin structure, while not yet disclosed in detail, should be additive given the technology-driven triage model.

**3. Medicare and International Expansion**

Hinge's fall prevention program for Medicare Advantage populations and its global program — now live in Canada, France, Germany, Ireland, the Netherlands, and the UK — represent optionality not captured in consensus estimates. The company currently has approximately 2.6 million contracted lives across fully-insured, Medicare Advantage, and federal channels, versus 22 million in self-insured. Closing even a fraction of that gap over five years would be transformative.

## Valuation: Paying a Reasonable Price for an Unreasonable Business

At approximately $41 per share, the market cap sits around $3.2 billion. Adjusting for $208 million in cash and $269 million in marketable securities, enterprise value is approximately $2.7 billion.

Against 2026 guidance:
- **Revenue guidance**: $732–$742 million (midpoint $737M) → **EV/Revenue: ~3.7x**
- **Non-GAAP operating income guidance**: $151–$156 million (midpoint ~$154M) → **EV/Non-GAAP EBIT: ~17.5x**
- **Implied FCF ~$170–$180 million** (assuming FCF margin of ~23–24%, slightly below 2025's 31% adjusted for IPO payroll tax benefit) → **EV/FCF: ~15–16x**

Management is guiding 25% revenue growth at the midpoint. For context: a business with 25% revenue growth, 83% gross margins, 20%+ operating margins, net dollar retention above 110%, and a dominant competitive position in a category Hinge's commissioned research sizes at $661 billion in annual U.S. direct spend would typically command 6–8x forward revenue. Hinge is trading below that threshold. I believe the discount reflects (1) healthcare regulatory risk, (2) GAAP loss confusion, and (3) post-IPO lock-up overhang noise — all of which are resolvable over the next 12–18 months.

## Key Risks

**Partner Concentration**: HCSC, Elevance, and Aetna together account for roughly 42% of revenue. Loss or renegotiation of any one would be materially disruptive. Per the 10-K, none of these agreements expire before 2027, but all are terminable for convenience — a latent risk investors should monitor.

**FDA Scrutiny of TrueMotion**: On April 22, 2025, the FDA sent Hinge a letter requesting information on whether TrueMotion requires clearance as a medical device. Management responded and expressed confidence in their regulatory position, but this remains an open question. An adverse determination could require a 510(k) or other regulatory submission and delay further development.

**Yield Growth Is Not Guaranteed**: The annual yield lever is compelling in theory, but moving a large, passive employer population to engage with voluntary physical therapy is genuinely hard. If yield stagnates at 3.9%, the near-term growth story depends more heavily on new client additions, which are inherently lumpier.

**Guidance Deceleration**: Management guided 2026 revenue growth of ~25% — a notable step down from 51% in 2025. They framed this as conservative, pointing to the selling season and 4.8 million net new contracted lives added. But investors will want to see Q1 and Q2 billings data to confirm the pipeline is converting.

**Dual-Class Structure**: Founders Daniel Perez and Gabriel Mecklenburg retain 15-vote Class B shares, concentrating governance control. Class A public shareholders have limited ability to influence corporate matters, including director elections.

## Why Now?

The stock has declined roughly 34% from its post-IPO high, yet the business has only gotten better. Q4 2025 non-GAAP operating margin hit 28% — already at the low end of the company's long-term 25%+ target — and management guided full-year 2026 margin of 21%, which implies intentional reinvestment rather than structural margin degradation.

The lock-up expiration noise is largely behind us. Insider selling pressure from pre-IPO holders has run its course, and the $250 million share repurchase authorization — with approximately $185 million remaining as of year-end 2025 — provides a meaningful technical bid.

The sector consolidation backdrop, with Sword/Kaia absorbing integration complexity and Omada finding its footing as a new public company, leaves Hinge uniquely positioned as the clean, profitable, pure-play option in digital MSK.

## Conclusion

Hinge Health's pullback from peak has created a valuation that materially underestimates both the durability of its competitive position and the optionality embedded in HingeSelect, Medicare, and international expansion. The business generated $180 million in free cash flow in its first full year of public life, with gross margins at 83% and a Rule of 40 score of 81 — metrics that would attract premium multiples in any sector. Trading at roughly 15–16x forward EV/FCF with 25% guided revenue growth and dominant market positioning, the risk/reward skews meaningfully to the upside for investors with a 2–3 year horizon.

The market is pricing a good business at a fair price. The data suggests this is a great business available at a good price.

**Rating: Bullish**
