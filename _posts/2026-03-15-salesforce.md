## A High-Quality Franchise at a Distressed Valuation

Salesforce (CRM) enters fiscal year 2027 with a peculiar combination of attributes that rarely coexist: record operating cash flow, accelerating AI product adoption, a contracting share count, and a stock that has lost roughly 29% over the past year. As of mid-March 2026, shares trade near $193 — well below their 52-week high above $310.

The bear case is well-rehearsed: AI-native challengers will disintermediate enterprise software; Agentforce is more marketing than revenue; organic growth is decelerating. Each concern has merit as a risk factor. None of them, in my assessment, adequately explains why a business generating $15 billion in annual operating cash flow should trade at roughly 24x trailing earnings.

I believe the market has conflated two distinct narratives — near-term organic revenue caution and long-term business model destruction — and is pricing Salesforce as if the second is inevitable. The fundamental evidence points to a business in the early stages of a meaningful AI-driven revenue cycle, backed by a capital return program of historic scale.

---

## What the Q4 FY2026 Numbers Actually Say

Salesforce delivered Q4 FY2026 revenue of $11.2 billion, up 12% year-over-year. Current remaining performance obligations (cRPO) — a forward-looking measure of contracted but unrecognized revenue due within 12 months — rose to $35.1 billion, up 16%. Total remaining performance obligations hit $72.4 billion, up 14%. These are not the numbers of a business under existential pressure.

Adjusted EPS of $3.81 topped consensus by roughly 25% — an exceptional beat. Revenue narrowly exceeded expectations. Yet the market sold off the stock, driven entirely by guidance optics.

For fiscal 2027, Salesforce guided revenue of $45.8 billion to $46.2 billion, implying 10–11% growth off its $41.5 billion FY26 base. This landed modestly below some Wall Street estimates, but the shortfall was immaterial in absolute terms. Investors had priced for acceleration, not continuation, and Salesforce delivered steady state.

**The organic growth caveat matters.** FY27 revenue guidance includes a contribution from the Informatica acquisition, meaning organic growth is likely in the high single digits. For a $41.5 billion revenue base, that is respectable — not transformative. The bull case requires Agentforce to bend the trajectory upward. Management has explicitly guided for this: the company said it expects organic revenue growth to reaccelerate in the second half of fiscal 2027.

## Agentforce: From Hype to Revenue Signal

The most important development in Salesforce's recent history is Agentforce — its platform for building and deploying autonomous AI agents within enterprise workflows. The debate is not whether the product exists but whether it will generate material incremental revenue at scale.

Q4 data provides the first real answer. According to Salesforce's earnings release:

- **Agentforce ARR reached $800 million**, up 169% year-over-year
- The company closed **29,000 deals**, up 50% quarter-over-quarter, encompassing approximately 22,000–23,000 customers
- **More than 75% of the top 100 Q4 wins** included both Agentforce and Data 360
- **Over 60% of Agentforce and Data 360 bookings** came from existing customers — expansion revenue with superior unit economics versus greenfield acquisition

Chief Revenue Officer Miguel Milano noted on the earnings call that Salesforce closed 12 deals above $10 million in the quarter. Three exceeded $20 million, and one reached approximately $50 million. Six of the 10 largest deals were upgrades of existing products, and seven of the top 10 deals included adding seats.

The deal velocity trajectory is striking. By Q3 FY26, Salesforce had reported more than 9,500 paid Agentforce deals. Three months later, the total reached 29,000. The company has consumed nearly 20 trillion tokens and converted them into more than 2.4 billion "agentic work units" — instances where AI delivered actual work output, not merely inference.

Real-world validation is emerging. The Adecco Group signed a multi-year agreement with Salesforce for unlimited global Agentforce 360 access, targeting over 50% of revenues powered by agentic AI by end of 2026. Salesforce EVP Madhav Thattai described the partnership as moving beyond experimentation to full-scale agentic enterprise deployment. These are not proofs of concept; they are production commitments.

**The $800 million ARR baseline is the number to watch.** At 169% year-over-year growth, even significant deceleration would imply a multi-billion dollar ARR product by FY28. Management has set a path to $63 billion in revenue by FY30, raised from the $60 billion target announced at the October 2025 Investor Day. That trajectory requires Agentforce to become a foundational revenue layer.

## The Informatica Integration: A Data Moat With Underappreciated Timing

Salesforce closed its acquisition of Informatica in November 2025. The deal's strategic logic deserves more attention than its financial mechanics.

Enterprise AI agents are only as effective as the data they can access. Informatica brings data cataloging, integration, governance, quality, privacy capabilities, metadata management, and master data management to the Salesforce platform. For large enterprises managing data across hybrid and multi-cloud environments — the norm, not the exception — Informatica solves the most common barrier to Agentforce deployment: data readiness.

The timing is deliberate. The most frequently cited impediment to enterprise AI adoption is data quality and governance. Successful deployment of Agentforce relies heavily on high-quality, well-governed data. Salesforce has effectively acquired the missing infrastructure layer precisely as demand for it peaks. As the integration matures, its value will be measured less in incremental Informatica revenue and more in the acceleration of Agentforce deal sizes, conversion rates, and consumption per customer.

## The $25 Billion Capital Return: Signal or Burden?

The most provocative recent development is Salesforce's decision to issue $25 billion in senior notes — its largest debt offering ever — entirely earmarked for an accelerated share repurchase.

According to SEC filings, on March 11, 2026, Salesforce entered accelerated share repurchase agreements with Banco Santander, Bank of America, Citibank, JPMorgan Chase, and Morgan Stanley to repurchase an aggregate of $25 billion of common stock. The company will receive approximately 80% of the expected shares on March 16, 2026, with final settlement in Q4 2026. The notes were issued in eight tranches maturing between 2028 and 2066.

Separately, the company executed a new $6 billion five-year senior unsecured term loan, using the proceeds to fully repay the existing $4 billion 364-day term loan and the $2 billion three-year Informatica term loan.

This $25 billion ASR follows $12.7 billion in buybacks already executed in FY26. Combined with the $50 billion total authorization announced in February, Salesforce is signaling extraordinary conviction that its own stock is mispriced. At ~$193 per share, this single transaction will retire roughly 14% of shares outstanding.

**The credit rating concern is real but manageable.** Moody's downgraded Salesforce one notch to A2, characterizing the debt-funded buyback as a material shift in financial policy with a higher tolerance for debt. S&P Global lowered its outlook to negative while affirming the A+ rating. Investors should monitor this: higher debt introduces financial risk if revenue growth disappoints materially.

However, context matters. Salesforce generated $15 billion in operating cash flow in FY26. With interest coverage ratios that most industrial companies would envy, the incremental debt is serviceable. This is not a leveraged buyout; it is a capital structure optimization by a business generating substantial, recurring cash flows.

## Valuation: What Is the Market Pricing In?

At $193, with shares outstanding in the low 930-million range, Salesforce's market cap is roughly $180 billion. For a software business with approximately 78% gross margins, around 20% GAAP operating margins, $72.4 billion in contracted obligations, and a nascent AI product growing at triple-digit rates, this is not obviously expensive.

A straightforward bull case:
- FY27 revenue of ~$46 billion at a 22% GAAP operating margin implies ~$10 billion in operating income
- Apply a 25x multiple — not demanding for a high-quality software franchise — yielding an enterprise value of $250 billion
- Adjust for cash and debt: a share price in the $230–$250 range

The bear case requires believing that Agentforce fails to convert pipeline into durable revenue, that organic growth remains stuck in the high single digits, and that AI proves more disruptive to the CRM layer than current evidence suggests. Each is a plausible outcome, but the probability of all three occurring simultaneously appears low at current prices.

## Key Risks

**1. Organic growth underwhelms in H1 FY27.** Management has guided for reacceleration in the second half. If cRPO growth does not inflect upward over the next two quarters, the thesis is delayed at best, impaired at worst.

**2. AI disruption accelerates.** The current consensus view among sell-side analysts is that AI model providers are unlikely to displace software incumbents and are instead positioning as orchestration layers atop existing systems. This supports Salesforce's positioning. A reversal — where AI bypasses the CRM layer entirely — would be structurally negative.

**3. Debt burden constrains flexibility.** Post-ASR, Salesforce will carry roughly $39 billion in gross debt. Based on FY26 cash flow generation, this is serviceable — but it significantly reduces the balance sheet flexibility that made Salesforce a powerful acquirer. Large-scale M&A is effectively off the table for several years.

**4. Agentforce pricing model friction.** Early challenges with the $2-per-conversation pricing model led to the introduction of a Flex Credits pay-per-action model at $0.10 per action. Consumption-based models introduce revenue volatility and complicate forecasting. If enterprise customers prove price-sensitive at scale, deal sizes could compress.

## Bottom Line

Salesforce is not a distressed business. It is a dominant enterprise software franchise with $72.4 billion in remaining performance obligations, approximately 78% gross margins, around 20% GAAP operating margins, and $15 billion in annual operating cash flow — executing a capital return program of historic magnitude at a depressed valuation.

Management framed the $50 billion repurchase authorization explicitly on the earnings call: *"Given the current stock price dislocation, the most prudent investment we can make is in Salesforce."* It is unusual for management to be this direct. It is now backing those words with $25 billion in bonds and an immediate ASR.

The investment case is not complicated. The market is pricing Salesforce as if the AI transition is a headwind. The evidence — 29,000 Agentforce deals, $800 million ARR growing 169% year-over-year, over 60% expansion bookings from existing customers — suggests it is a tailwind still in early innings.

Patient investors with a 12–18 month horizon get a high-quality business at trough valuation, a rapidly growing AI revenue layer approaching materiality, an Informatica integration that strengthens the data moat at exactly the right moment, and a share count declining at an accelerating rate. That is a compelling setup.

**CRM is appropriate for investors seeking quality software exposure at value multiples, with a 12–18 month horizon and tolerance for continued near-term volatility.**
