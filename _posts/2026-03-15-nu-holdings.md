# Nu Holdings: A $67 Billion Bank Trading at a Discount to Its Own Growth Story

**The stock has shed 27% from its January high. The fundamentals argue for re-engagement.**

---

Nu Holdings (NYSE: NU) hit an all-time high near $18.98 in late January 2026 and has since retreated roughly 27% to approximately $13.89. The selloff reflects a strengthening dollar, macro nervousness around Brazil, and broad emerging-market risk-off sentiment. None of that changes the underlying story: this is a hyper-profitable, structurally advantaged digital bank compounding earnings at 45%+ annually, trading at roughly 23x trailing earnings in the midst of a multi-year global expansion.

Nu is not merely a growth story — it is an operating leverage story, with a cost structure that no incumbent bank in its markets can match. The key question for investors today is whether the pullback represents a macro-induced entry point or a harbinger of genuine earnings deterioration. After examining the Q4 2025 results, the company's regulatory evolution, and its US expansion optionality, the evidence tilts firmly toward the former.

---

## Q4 2025: Inflection, Not Deceleration

Nu's Q4 2025 earnings, reported February 25, were operationally exceptional and perhaps underappreciated by the market.

Full-year 2025 revenue came in at approximately $15.9 billion — up about 43% from 2024 — while net income reached $2.87 billion, nearly 46% growth year over year. The quarterly trajectory matters most: Q4 net income was $895 million, up from $553 million in Q4 2024.

The efficiency ratio — how much of revenue is consumed by operating expenses — stood at just **20%** in Q4 2025, down from 22% in Q4 2024 and a staggering 78% back in Q4 2021. For context, Brazil's Itaú Unibanco, one of the most efficient traditional banks in Latin America, has recently reported efficiency ratios in the high 30s to low 40s. Nu's ratio is roughly half that — a structural advantage that flows directly from having no branch network and running a fully cloud-native platform.

Revenue composition is evolving favorably:

- **Credit income**: $2,778 million in Q4 2025 (57% of total)
- **Float income**: $1,409 million (29%)
- **Fee income**: $670 million (14%)

Credit income growth reflects a loan portfolio that grew 40% year-over-year on a foreign exchange-neutral (FXN) basis to $32.7 billion, with secured loans up 71% FXN and unsecured loans up 55% FXN. Credit card balances grew 32% FXN. The mix shift toward higher-yielding lending is deliberate.

**Return on equity** closed Q4 at 33% annualized, rising from 29% a year ago. Adjusted ROE hit 35%. These are world-class numbers for any bank, developed or emerging market.

## The ARPAC Flywheel — Still Early Innings

The single most underrated metric in Nu's model is Monthly Average Revenue Per Active Customer (ARPAC). This metric reveals the depth of the customer relationship, and the trend confirms a genuine financial services flywheel at work.

ARPAC grew to $15.00 in Q4 2025, up 25% FXN year-over-year. Cost to serve per active customer remained flat at $0.80. The widening gap between these two numbers — essentially the unit contribution before credit losses — is the engine of Nu's profitability expansion.

What makes this especially compelling is the cohort data. Nu's own presentations show that a customer active for 12 months generates roughly $4.80 per month in revenue. By 96 months, that figure rises to $30.20. The current average ARPAC of $15.00 implies the installed base sits somewhere around the 60–72 month maturity range — meaning the majority of customers still have years of revenue deepening ahead.

With 131 million total customers and an activity rate of 86% in Brazil, Nu keeps users engaged at remarkably high rates. The average number of products per active customer reached 4.1 by year-end 2024. The strategic imperative for 2026 — growing wallet share, ARPAC, and product penetration — is well-defined and has proven executable.

## Credit Quality: Managed, Not Broken

Bears frequently point to Nu's non-performing loan ratios as a structural concern. This deserves honest treatment.

The 90+ day NPL ratio stood at 6.7% as of Q4 2024. The 15–90 day NPL ratio improved to 4.1% by Q4 2025. The coverage ratio over 90+ day NPLs was approximately 227% in Q4 2024, meaning Nu holds more than twice the reserves necessary to cover its most troubled loans.

Several contextual points are essential:

1. **Customer mix matters.** Nu serves a disproportionate share of first-time borrowers and thin-file customers — people who had no credit card before joining Nu. Elevated NPLs are a mathematical feature of serving this population, not necessarily a sign of underwriting failure.

2. **Risk-adjusted NIM is recovering.** After dipping to 8.2% in Q1 2025 due to a step-up in provision charges, the risk-adjusted net interest margin recovered to 10.5% in Q4 2025, demonstrating that Nu's underwriting and pricing machine is working.

3. **Renegotiated balances are stabilizing.** The proportion of renegotiated balances falling more than 90 days past due declined from 32% in Q3 2024 to 29% in Q4 2025, suggesting improving resolution rates.

The credit story is not without risk — Brazil's elevated Selic rate creates genuine consumer stress — but it is neither as alarming as bears suggest nor as clean as bulls might wish.

## Three Regulatory Catalysts the Market Has Not Fully Priced

The most underappreciated aspect of Nu's 2026 investment case is a simultaneous convergence of regulatory upgrades across three jurisdictions, each expanding the company's addressable product set.

### Brazil: Full Banking License

Nu announced in late 2025 its intention to obtain a full banking license in Brazil. The practical near-term impact is modest — Nu already operates under payment institution, credit company, and brokerage licenses — but a bank charter unlocks expanded product categories, deeper participation in interbank markets, and higher deposit insurance limits that could accelerate an already-growing deposit base. Total deposits reached $41.9 billion at Q4 2025, up from $28.9 billion a year earlier. Importantly, the company has indicated that the license upgrade does not materially alter capital and liquidity requirements — it is additive without being dilutive.

### Mexico: Banking License in Progress

Nu Mexico received authorization from the CNBV (Mexico's banking regulator) in April 2025 to organize as a banking institution and awaits final operational approval. Mexico is deeply underpenetrated — just 15.7% of adults aged 18–70 held a bank-issued credit card as of 2024. Nu had 14 million customers in Mexico at Q4 2025, representing approximately 15% of the adult population. A full banking license allows deposit-taking at scale and a more complete product suite, replicating the trajectory that worked so effectively in Brazil.

### US: The Long Game

On January 29, 2026, Nu received conditional approval from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) to form a de novo national bank, Nubank, N.A. The US organization will be led by co-founder Cristina Junqueira, who has relocated to the United States to spearhead the effort. Roberto Campos Neto, former president of the Central Bank of Brazil, will serve as chairman of the board.

Nu has entered the bank organization phase, which requires satisfying specific OCC conditions and securing pending approvals from the FDIC and the Federal Reserve. Regulatory requirements dictate that the company must fully capitalize the institution within 12 months and open the bank within 18 months.

The US is not a near-term earnings driver. It is better understood as an embedded option — one that costs the company relatively little to pursue given its capital position but that could be worth considerably more than zero if it captures even a fraction of the underserved banking population. As CEO David Vélez put it: "This approval isn't just an expansion of our operation; it's an opportunity to prove our thesis that a digital-first, customer-centric model is the future of financial services globally."

## Valuation: Cheap for the First Time in Two Years

At $13.89, Nu trades at approximately 23.5x trailing earnings. The consensus analyst target of $20.48 implies roughly 47% upside from current levels.

The right framework for valuing Nu is not a static multiple but a forward earnings trajectory discounted against its growth and ROE profile.

| Year | Consensus Revenue | Consensus Net Income | EPS (Avg) |
|------|------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| 2026E | $21.1B | $4.14B | $0.87 |
| 2027E | $25.5B | $5.49B | $1.15 |
| 2028E | $30.1B | $7.47B | $1.48 |

At the current price and 2027 consensus EPS of $1.15, NU trades at approximately 12x forward 2027 earnings — for a company generating 33%+ ROE and growing net income at roughly 35–50% annually. That is an unusual combination.

**Comps context:** MercadoLibre, the most obvious Latin American digital platform comparator, trades at about 42x trailing earnings. Nu sits well below that, arguably deserving a premium given its growth rate but not the stratosphere of pure-growth fintech multiples.

A reasonable valuation framework: apply a 20x multiple to 2026E EPS of $0.87 and you get approximately $17.40. Apply a 20x multiple to 2027E EPS of $1.15 and you reach $23.00. Neither scenario requires heroic assumptions about the US business or Colombia — these are essentially base-case, Latin America-only valuations with no credit assigned to the US option.

## What Could Go Wrong

Bulls need to honestly confront several risks:

**Brazilian macro and currency.** The vast majority of Nu's revenue comes from Brazil. The real remains volatile, and FX translation into USD creates meaningful reported earnings swings even when the underlying business performs well in local-currency terms.

**Consumer credit cycle.** Brazil's elevated nominal interest rates pressure consumer balance sheets. If unemployment rises or consumer confidence deteriorates, NPL formation could spike. Nu is not immune to credit cycles; it simply manages them more nimbly than traditional banks through real-time data feedback loops and its proprietary credit engine.

**Competition deepening.** Mercado Pago — the financial arm of MercadoLibre — is Nu's most credible competitor in Latin America. Both are competing aggressively for the same digitally native customer. Traditional banks have also invested heavily in digital transformation. Nu's moat is real but not impenetrable.

**US execution risk.** There is genuine execution risk if Nu struggles to meet OCC, FDIC, and Federal Reserve conditions on time, which could delay the US launch or require changes to its planned product set. Regulatory and compliance costs, layered on top of already rigorous requirements in Brazil and Mexico, could weigh on profitability if US revenue ramps slowly.

**Valuation re-rating risk.** If emerging-market risk appetite deteriorates further — driven by dollar strength, US tariff uncertainty, or Brazil-specific political risk — multiples on Brazilian digital assets can compress sharply, regardless of fundamentals.

## The Bottom Line

Nu Holdings is a rare combination: a genuinely world-class business model, in an underpenetrated market, at a valuation that has been meaningfully compressed by macro factors rather than fundamental deterioration. The 27% pullback from the January high has brought the forward multiple to levels not seen in roughly two years.

The stock is appropriate for investors with a 2–3 year time horizon who can tolerate emerging-market currency and macro volatility. The company is scaling into multiple new revenue categories — secured lending, payroll loans, insurance, investments, SME credit — while simultaneously deepening the core credit card and personal loan franchise. The US charter is a free option on top of an already-compelling Latin American story.

The base case: Nu compounds net income at 35–45% annually through 2027, reaches approximately $4+ billion in net income in 2026, and the stock re-rates toward $20–23 as execution consistency reduces the discount investors apply to emerging-market growth. That represents a 45–65% return from current levels before any US optionality is assigned.

For growth-oriented investors willing to weather emerging-market volatility, the current entry point is among the more attractive Nu has offered in the past two years.
