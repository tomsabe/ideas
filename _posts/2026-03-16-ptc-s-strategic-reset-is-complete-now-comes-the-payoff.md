March 16, 2026, marks a genuine inflection point for PTC.

The company has completed the sale of its Kepware industrial connectivity and ThingWorx Internet of Things businesses to TPG. CEO Neil Barua framed the move as enabling PTC to "increase our focus on our Intelligent Product Lifecycle vision." This was not a forced divestiture — it was a deliberate, multi-year strategic choice that now deserves a fresh look from investors who have been waiting for clarity.

The stock is down roughly 24% over the past six months, trading around $156 against an analyst consensus target of approximately $205. That gap reflects genuine uncertainty about what post-divestiture PTC looks like as a standalone business. I believe that uncertainty has now been resolved, that the resulting entity is structurally superior to what preceded it, and that the valuation has not yet caught up.

## What PTC Sold — and Why It Was Smart

PTC received cash proceeds of $523 million at closing, reflecting $42 million of adjustments related to working capital and indebtedness. Net after-tax proceeds are approximately $375 million — after roughly $40 million in costs and $110 million in cash taxes — and will fund share repurchases, including a $375 million accelerated share repurchase program.

The strategic logic deserves unpacking. Kepware and ThingWorx contributed approximately $160 million in FY2025 annual recurring revenue (ARR), with constant-currency ARR growth of negative 1%, and approximately $70 million in free cash flow. These were low-growth, capital-consuming assets that diluted the quality of PTC's subscription portfolio. Selling them at roughly 7–8x free cash flow was rational capital allocation. PTC also retains potential contingent consideration of up to $125 million, payable upon TPG's future sale of the divested businesses — an additional upside option.

The market penalized PTC for the headline revenue reduction, but this confuses accounting optics with business quality. What remains is what PTC does best.

## The Post-Divestiture Business: A Vertically Integrated Industrial Software Platform

PTC is now a four-product-line company: **CAD** (Creo, Onshape), **PLM** (Windchill, Arena, FlexPLM), **ALM** (Codebeamer, pure::variants), and **SLM** (ServiceMax, Servigistics).

Consider a manufacturer designing a new medical device: engineers create the product in Creo, manage documentation and collaborative workflows in Windchill, coordinate embedded software development through Codebeamer, and run field service operations with ServiceMax — all within an integrated ecosystem maintaining a single source of truth for product data. This is not a collection of point solutions. It is a vertically integrated data platform for discrete manufacturing, with deep switching costs at each node.

**The ARR trajectory is healthy.** In Q1 FY2026 (ended December 31, 2025), reported ARR reached $2,494 million, a 13% increase year-over-year. Stripping out Kepware and ThingWorx, the core growth rate was stronger still. Updated FY2026 ARR guidance, excluding divested businesses, calls for constant-currency growth of 7.5% to 9.5%. Over 95% of PTC's revenue is recurring — a subscription-driven franchise with high visibility.

**Margins have structurally re-rated.** For fiscal year 2025 (ended September 30, 2025):

| Metric | FY2025 | Change |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | $2.74 billion | +19% YoY |
| Gross margin | 84% | — |
| GAAP operating margin | 36% | Up from 26% |
| Non-GAAP operating margin | 48% | — |
| Free cash flow | $857 million | +16% YoY |
| Diluted EPS | $6.08 | +95% YoY |

These are the financials of a business that has largely completed its transition from perpetual licenses to subscriptions and is now harvesting the operating leverage embedded in that model. FY2026 cash flow guidance includes approximately $150 million of non-recurring divestiture-related outflows. Once those wash through, I believe PTC is approaching $1 billion in annual free cash flow from a cleaner, higher-margin base.

## Capital Return: The Underappreciated Catalyst

The most underappreciated element of the PTC thesis today is the sheer scale of capital being returned. In FY2026, PTC expects to repurchase approximately $1.125 billion to $1.325 billion of its shares. Against a current market cap of roughly $18.6 billion, that implies a buyback yield of 6–7% in a single fiscal year.

The mechanics: PTC intends to enter a $375 million accelerated share repurchase agreement in Q2 FY2026, with final settlement expected in Q3 FY2026. Additionally, the company plans to repurchase approximately $250 million of common stock in Q2, with net after-tax divestiture proceeds funding incremental repurchases through the ASR program. Fully diluted shares are expected to decrease to approximately 118 million in Q2 FY2026, down from 121 million a year earlier.

This matters for earnings math. Even with somewhat lower near-term revenue from divested businesses, GAAP EPS guidance has actually risen — driven by a $464 million gain on sale and the significant share count compression from planned repurchases. This is a genuine earnings accretion story.

## Competitive Position and the AI Angle

PTC competes in a concentrated market for enterprise industrial software. Its primary rivals in CAD and PLM include Siemens (Teamcenter, NX), Dassault Systèmes (CATIA, ENOVIA/3DEXPERIENCE), and Autodesk. PTC differentiates by focusing on the "nerve center" of PLM and ALM — emphasizing how software embeds into product development cycles and democratizing product data across the enterprise — rather than competing in deeper manufacturing execution where some rivals are more heavily invested.

This positioning is defensible. Windchill is deeply embedded in the engineering workflows of aerospace, defense, automotive, and medical device manufacturers globally. Once a large manufacturer has built its bill-of-materials management, change control, and configuration management processes around Windchill, migrating to a competitor is a multi-year, enormously expensive undertaking.

The AI angle is real, not marketing spin. PTC has been methodically embedding AI into its core products: Windchill AI Parts Rationalization (released January 2026) helps manufacturers eliminate duplicate parts and improve data consistency; Codebeamer AI capabilities were introduced in early 2026; and Onshape launched cloud-native Model-Based Definition in February 2026. These are not standalone products — they are AI layers built into systems engineers already use daily. The adoption barrier is minimal when the intelligence is embedded in the tool already open on the engineer's desktop.

PTC has also expanded its collaboration with NVIDIA, integrating Omniverse into Creo and Windchill — positioning PTC's product data as connective tissue for the emerging intersection of industrial software and AI-driven simulation.

## Valuation: Discount to Intrinsic Value

At $156 per share, PTC trades at approximately 22.7x trailing GAAP earnings and roughly 16x trailing EV/EBITDA. On a forward basis using consensus FY2026 EPS of approximately $8.09, the stock trades at roughly 19x forward earnings — before accounting for the full impact of share count compression from the buyback program.

The more appropriate valuation framework for a business with 84% gross margins, 95%+ recurring revenue, and approximately $900–950 million in normalized annual free cash flow (adjusting FY2026's roughly $850 million guidance for non-recurring divestiture costs) is EV/FCF. On that basis, PTC trades at roughly 21–22x normalized free cash flow. That is not cheap in absolute terms, but it represents a meaningful discount relative to the quality of the business and to the analyst consensus target of approximately $205, which implies roughly 31% upside.

With $1.1–1.3 billion in buybacks compressing the share count significantly over the next 12 months, per-share free cash flow accretion alone argues for a re-rating.

## Risks Worth Monitoring

**Macro and trade exposure.** PTC's own risk disclosures flag the possibility that macroeconomic conditions may deteriorate due to factors including import tariffs and global trade tensions, potentially causing customers to delay or reduce purchases. Approximately 36% of revenue comes from Europe, with Germany a particularly significant market. A prolonged slowdown in European manufacturing — already visible in the automotive sector — is a genuine headwind.

**Currency sensitivity.** Approximately 50% of PTC's revenue is transacted in non-dollar currencies, with the euro representing the most significant single exposure. A materially stronger dollar would pressure reported ARR growth even if underlying demand holds.

**Go-to-market execution.** PTC undertook a significant go-to-market realignment in FY2025, reorganizing around vertical industries. Management pointed to record Q1 FY2026 large-deal volume and improved seller productivity as evidence this is working. Sustained execution across the full sales cycle is not yet proven.

**AI commoditization.** If AI capabilities become standardized across the CAD/PLM market, PTC's ability to differentiate narrows. This is a longer-dated risk, but it is real.

## Conclusion

PTC today is a simpler, higher-quality business than it was 18 months ago. The IoT portfolio overhang is gone. The go-to-market transformation is past its heaviest investment phase. The core business generates 8–9% ARR growth, operates at 84% gross margins, and is on track to produce close to $1 billion in normalized free cash flow annually from a clean post-divestiture base. The company is returning over $1 billion to shareholders in FY2026 alone, compressing a share count that will make per-share earnings accretion visible by mid-year.

At roughly 21–22x normalized free cash flow and a meaningful discount to consensus price targets, PTC offers a risk-adjusted entry point for investors willing to look past the divestiture-related guidance noise and focus on what the business will look like in FY2027 and beyond. The transformation is not forthcoming — it is complete.
