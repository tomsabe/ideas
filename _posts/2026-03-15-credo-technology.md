## The Market's Overreaction Creates an Opportunity

Credo's fiscal Q3 2026 earnings report (for the quarter ended January 31, 2026) was, by any reasonable measure, extraordinary. Revenue hit $407.0 million, up 201.5% year-over-year and 51.9% sequentially, with a GAAP gross margin of 68.5% and non-GAAP net income of $208.8 million. Yet shares dropped sharply the day after — roughly 15% — and the selloff has since deepened, pushing the stock more than 45% below its 52-week high of approximately $214.

The market's concern is legitimate but, in my view, overpriced: Q4 guidance implies a sequential gross margin step-down, and fiscal 2027 growth, while still exceptional in absolute terms, will decelerate sharply from the tripling-of-revenue pace set in fiscal 2026. The sell-off treats a deceleration as a deterioration. That conflation is the opportunity.

At $117.69 (as of March 16, 2026), Credo's enterprise value sits around $20.5 billion. Against consensus fiscal 2027 revenue of approximately $2.03 billion, that implies roughly 10x forward EV/Sales — a meaningful compression from the 20x-plus the stock commanded near its peak, and a multiple that looks increasingly reasonable for a business with this growth and margin profile.

## Why Credo's Technology Is Hard to Replicate

Credo designs high-speed connectivity chips and cables for AI data centers. Its flagship — the HiWire Active Electrical Cable (AEC) — is a copper cable assembly with integrated signal-conditioning circuitry that connects GPUs within and across racks.

The key differentiator is not speed; it's reliability. CEO Bill Brennan has described AECs as increasingly displacing laser-based optical modules for intra-rack and rack-to-rack links up to 7 meters, with Credo's ZeroFlap AECs delivering up to "1,000 times better reliability" than commodity laser-based optics while consuming roughly half the power.

That reliability advantage has quantifiable economic value. In clusters with 100,000 or more GPUs, a single faulty link — called a "link flap" — can crash an entire AI training run, forcing a costly reload from a checkpoint. Reliability is not a marketing claim; it's a hard economic benefit measured in compute uptime.

Credo's proprietary SerDes (Serializer/Deserializer) and DSP (Digital Signal Processor) architecture, refined since the company's founding in 2008, underpins this performance advantage.

## A Quarter That Rewrote the Revenue Curve

The sequential leap — from $268 million in Q2 to $407 million in Q3 — reflects an acceleration in hyperscaler deployment, not a one-time pull-forward. The company's 10-Q filing shows its largest single customer accounted for 39% of Q3 revenue, down from 67% of total fiscal 2025 revenue according to the company's annual report. That organic diversification is material.

Full-year fiscal 2026 revenue is now projected to exceed $1.3 billion — more than tripling the prior year.

**Q4 fiscal 2026 guidance** is constructive but includes a notable margin step-down: revenue of $425–$435 million, non-GAAP gross margin of 64–66%, non-GAAP operating expenses of $76–$80 million, and a diluted weighted-average share count of approximately 197 million shares. The margin guide — down from the 68.6% non-GAAP gross margin posted in Q3 — is the proximate cause of the post-earnings selloff.

CFO Daniel Fleming acknowledged that "gross margin expansion will not always be linear as we continue to increase scale. There will always be differences from quarter to quarter in product mix." This is partly a product-mix phenomenon — AECs at high volumes carry lower margins than discrete ICs — and partly a function of new product ramp costs. It is not a structural deterioration.

## Fiscal 2027: 50%+ Growth From a $1.3 Billion Base

The bear narrative after earnings fixated on decelerating growth. The bull counter is that "deceleration" into 50%-plus growth from a $1.3 billion revenue base is an unusual gift in public markets.

Fleming stated on the Q3 call that the company expects "sequential revenue growth in the mid-single digits, leading to more than 50% year-over-year growth" in fiscal 2027. On a consensus fiscal 2026 base of approximately $1.33 billion, that implies fiscal 2027 revenue approaching $2.0 billion — consistent with the $2.03 billion analyst consensus.

The key catalyst: Credo pulled forward the ZeroFlap Optics production ramp to fiscal Q1 2027, earlier than the second-half fiscal 2027 timeline previously signaled. As of Q3, the company had one production customer for ZeroFlap optics plus three additional customers in qualification. This timeline acceleration means incremental optical revenue begins contributing a full quarter earlier than the market had modeled.

## Five Pillars, Not One: The TAM Expansion Story

The market prices Credo as an AEC company with a lumpy hyperscaler revenue profile. The more accurate characterization is a five-pillar connectivity platform where several pillars are still pre-revenue or in early production.

Brennan has outlined these five pillars — AECs, IC solutions (including retimers and optical DSPs), ZeroFlap Optics, Active LED Cables (ALCs), and OmniConnect gearbox solutions — with a combined total market opportunity management believes will exceed $10 billion in the coming years.

**ZeroFlap Optics:** Optical transceivers engineered with the same reliability-first architecture as Credo's AECs. At OFC 2026 in March, the company demonstrated 400G and 800G ZeroFlap optical transceivers in a live AI network fabric with real-time link telemetry powered by its PILOT monitoring platform, alongside a 1.6T LRO module based on its 3nm Bluebird DSP.

**Active LED Cables (ALCs):** These use microLED emitters to achieve optical connectivity with copper-like reliability and reach of up to 30 meters for row-scale AI networks. ALCs leverage microLED expertise from Credo's Hyperlume acquisition. The company expects to sample and qualify its first ALC products in fiscal 2027, with a production ramp in fiscal 2028. Management has stated the ALC total addressable market will ultimately be more than double the AEC TAM.

**OmniConnect:** This product line combines Credo's purpose-built VSR SerDes with a family of gearboxes for XPU connectivity. The first product, Weaver, enables up to a 10x improvement in memory beachfront I/O density with reach up to 10 inches. By converting VSR to DDR, Weaver overcomes the physical fan-out constraints of traditional memory-to-compute interconnects. Customer Positron plans to use the architecture in an inference XPU design, with a production ramp expected in fiscal 2028.

The revenue contribution timeline — Optics beginning in fiscal 2027, ALCs and OmniConnect in fiscal 2028 — means the current valuation gives investors the existing $1.3 billion-plus business along with optionality on additional multi-billion dollar TAM expansions at a price that already discounts significant growth deceleration.

## The CoMira Acquisition: A Strategic Layer Add

On March 2, 2026, Credo announced the acquisition of CoMira Solutions, bringing specialized link layer, error correction, and security semiconductor IP for next-generation AI architectures. CoMira's team has deployed IP in high-volume chips optimized for networking, AI, high-performance computing, and servers.

The strategic significance lies in protocol positioning. CoMira's IP supports Ethernet, ESUN, UALink, and PCIe — with UALink and ESUN being emerging scale-up interconnect standards developed by hyperscaler consortia as alternatives to Nvidia's NVLink. Credo is not merely riding existing standards; it is positioning at the protocol level as the AI networking stack evolves. In January 2026, the company unveiled the Blue Heron 224G AI scale-up retimer — a multiprotocol device supporting UALink, ESUN, and Ethernet, built on an advanced 3nm process with Credo's 224G SerDes, enabling full recovery of a 40+dB 224G link for rack-scale AI architectures.

## Customer Concentration: Real Risk, Improving Trajectory

No honest analysis of Credo ignores customer concentration. In Q3 fiscal 2026, the top three customers accounted for approximately 88% of revenue, per the company's 10-Q filing. That is real. A hyperscaler capex pause or architectural shift could compress near-term revenue materially and quickly.

But the trajectory is improving. In fiscal 2025, one customer represented 67% of total revenue. By Q3 fiscal 2026, the largest single customer was down to 39%. Management expects three to four customers to each contribute over 10% of revenue in coming quarters, and Brennan confirmed new wins including a fifth hyperscaler.

The diversification is structural — Credo's reliability advantage is sufficiently strong that additional hyperscalers are adopting without price concessions. The concentration risk is declining, not static.

## Valuation: Compressed Multiple, Expanding Opportunity

At roughly 10x forward EV/Sales on consensus fiscal 2027 revenue, the multiple has compressed sharply from peak levels. For a business that posted a non-GAAP operating margin of approximately 50% in Q3 fiscal 2026 and is guiding to 50%-plus revenue growth in fiscal 2027, that ratio implies an EV/operating-income multiple in a range that I believe is defensible given the secular tailwinds and expanding product optionality.

The bear case requires believing that: (1) hyperscaler AEC adoption has peaked; (2) ZeroFlap Optics fails to ramp; and (3) Credo loses share to Broadcom, Marvell, or Astera Labs. None of these is implausible, but at current prices, the market appears to be pricing in too much of all three simultaneously.

The bull case requires simply that the AEC franchise maintains its trajectory, the ZeroFlap Optics ramp materializes on the pulled-forward schedule, and one or two of the newer pillars contributes material revenue by fiscal 2028.

## Key Risks

**Hyperscaler capex concentration.** A pause in spending by Credo's anchor customers could produce a sharp, sudden revenue miss. This is not theoretical — it has happened before.

**Gross margin normalization.** The Q4 fiscal 2026 guide of 64–66% and management's longer-term "mid-60s" framing suggest the Q3 peak of 68.6% was mix-driven. As AEC volumes scale, blended margins may face structural pressure.

**Co-packaged optics displacement.** The most cited bear case is that co-packaged optics — which integrate optics directly into switch ASICs, potentially bypassing AECs and pluggable optics — could displace Credo's products over a 3–5 year horizon. Brennan has acknowledged the market is moving toward a "heterogeneous mix" of copper, pluggable optics, near-package optics, and eventually CPO, but argues reliability remains a gating factor for broader adoption of newer optical architectures.

**Geopolitical and tariff risk.** Fleming noted the Q4 outlook is based on the "current tariff regime," which he described as fluid. With manufacturing through TSMC in Taiwan and over half of fiscal 2025 revenue flowing through Hong Kong entities, Credo carries material geopolitical exposure.

**Single-source manufacturing.** Credo exclusively uses TSMC for semiconductor wafer fabrication. Any supply disruption — natural disaster, geopolitical escalation, or capacity reallocation — would be immediately impactful.

## Buy the Pullback, Own the Platform

Credo's 45% drawdown is not a verdict on the business — it is a sentiment correction from an unsustainably euphoric valuation. Brennan affirmed confidence in meeting supply requirements across all product categories, calling supply chain execution a "distinct competitive advantage."

The core thesis is straightforward. Credo owns a defensible technology advantage in AI data center interconnect, rooted in proprietary SerDes and DSP IP refined over nearly two decades. The AEC business alone — growing into a 1.6T transition and expanding to a fifth hyperscaler — supports the current valuation. The newer growth pillars — ZeroFlap Optics, ALCs, and OmniConnect — represent multi-billion dollar addressable markets where Credo has early-mover advantage and active customer engagements.

For investors with a 12–24 month horizon who can tolerate hyperscaler-concentration volatility, CRDO at current levels is a compelling accumulation opportunity in one of the few semiconductor companies simultaneously executing on a $1.3 billion revenue base and seeding the next wave of AI infrastructure connectivity.
