## A Complicated Story, Brutally Simplified

Cogent Communications Holdings (CCOI) has been one of the most punishing trades in telecom over the past year. Through early December 2025, shares had fallen roughly 75% from the start of the year, plunging well below the 52-week high near $83–$84. As of March 21, 2026, the stock sits around $18—a multi-year low that has wiped out years of dividend-reinvested gains for long-term holders.

The collapse was triggered by a confluence of visible but underappreciated forces: hemorrhaging legacy Sprint wireline revenue, a 98% dividend cut that ended a roughly 13-year growth streak, negative operating cash flow, and a looming $750 million debt maturity in June 2027 with no refinancing yet in place.

But beneath that wreckage is a set of assets and cash flow streams that, properly framed, make Cogent one of the more interesting risk/reward situations in telecom infrastructure. The thesis is not for the faint-hearted.

---

## Two Businesses Running in Opposite Directions

The single most important fact about Cogent today: it is not one business—it is two, moving in diametrically opposite directions.

**The Legacy Sprint Wireline Book (Dying):** Revenue from the acquired Sprint wireline contracts has collapsed from $118 million per quarter at deal close in May 2023 to approximately $43 million per quarter in Q4 2025—a 64% decline. This was always the expected trajectory; Cogent acquired a distressed book of low-margin enterprise and non-core wireline contracts it had little interest in perpetuating. Sprint-acquired revenues now represent roughly 18% of total company revenue, down from 42% at closing.

**The Legacy Cogent Business (Growing):** Meanwhile, Cogent's original business—IP transit, on-net corporate connections, and now wavelength services—has grown from $155 million to $197 million per quarter over the same period. This is the business Cogent built over two decades: low-cost, high-margin internet backbone services delivered over a purpose-built, all-optical network.

The headline revenue figure—$240.5 million for Q4 2025, down 4.7% year-over-year, and $975.8 million for the full year versus $1.036 billion in 2024—is the sum of these two vectors. The key question: when does the growth side overtake the dying side? Management believes that inflection has already begun.

---

## The Wavelength Opportunity: Real, But Still Small

The most consequential strategic development since the Sprint acquisition is Cogent's emergence as an optical wavelength provider—selling dedicated, high-capacity light paths over fiber to enterprises and hyperscalers. This was not possible without the Sprint network's roughly 23,500 route miles of owned long-haul and metro fiber.

The numbers are growing rapidly from a small base. Wavelength revenue rose 18.8% sequentially to $12.1 million in Q4 2025, up 73.7% year-over-year. Full-year 2025 wavelength revenue doubled to $38.5 million. Customer connections reached 2,064, up 84.6% from a year earlier.

The competitive case rests on several pillars: unique fiber routes along railroad rights-of-way (deeply buried, fewer cuts), a footprint spanning 1,068 wave-enabled locations across the U.S., Canada, and Mexico, faster provisioning (typically under 30 business days versus industry norms that run meaningfully longer), and aggressive pricing at roughly a 20%–30% discount to incumbents.

Management notes Cogent holds about 2% market share in wavelength—compared with 25% in transit and 35% in its multi-tenant office building business—and that on-net products carry contribution margins above 90%. The North American intercity wavelength market is estimated at approximately $2 billion annually. Even modest share capture at these margins is materially needle-moving.

**The bull case:** Hyperscalers, AI data center operators, and large enterprises increasingly need dedicated optical transport. Cogent owns a nationally contiguous fiber network acquired for a purchase price of $1 and is pricing aggressively, targeting 25% market share over time.

**The bear case:** At $38.5 million for all of 2025, wavelength revenue remains tiny relative to the $976 million revenue base. Incumbents like Zayo and Lumen have decades of customer relationships and brand recognition. Landing large contracts is a long sales cycle against well-entrenched competition.

## IPv4 Leasing: The Overlooked Annuity

One asset that deserves more investor attention is the IPv4 address portfolio. Cogent owns approximately 38 million IPv4 addresses—the majority predating the Sprint deal, with about 9.9 million acquired as part of that transaction—and has built a separate securitized vehicle (Cogent IPv4 LLC) to monetize them.

Revenue from leasing IPv4 addresses grew 43.8% year-over-year to $64.5 million for 2025. This is high-margin, recurring revenue backed by a finite, globally scarce asset: the world's supply of IPv4 addresses is exhausted, and full transition to IPv6 remains years away. The company has financed this business with $380.4 million of ring-fenced, asset-backed notes secured by the IPv4 addresses—structurally separate from the operating company's debt.

The balance sheet carries the IPv4 intangible at $458 million, assessed annually for impairment without cause for a write-down. At $64.5 million in annual lease revenue and growing, the asset generates a respectable yield and functions as a hidden stabilizer in the capital structure.

## The Debt Refinancing: Existential Risk Becoming Manageable

The single largest overhang on CCOI equity is $750 million of 7.00% senior unsecured notes maturing in June 2027. If not refinanced by approximately June 2026, those notes become a current liability on the balance sheet—a classification that would further spook an already skittish investor base.

Management has been explicit about its plan. CEO Dave Schaeffer said the company cut its dividend by 98% to delever after leverage rose to 6.6x. Cogent's debt stack also includes $600 million of 6.5% senior secured notes maturing in 2032.

The refinancing blueprint, laid out in detail at the J.P. Morgan Credit Conference in early March 2026, involves several interlocking steps:

- **Restructuring:** Moving approximately $569 million of IRU-backed obligations (long-term capacity contracts) into a separate infrastructure entity, freeing capacity at the operating company.
- **Secured replacement:** Refinancing the $750 million unsecured notes with $750 million of new secured debt, ranking pari passu with the existing 2032 secured bonds. This would place pro forma secured leverage at 3.91x against a 4x test—tight, but within covenant bounds.
- **Maturity management:** Temporarily extending the existing secured bonds by one year so the new bonds mature at least one year later.
- **Asset sales:** A pending, non-binding letter of intent to sell 10 data centers to a major global infrastructure fund for proceeds described as "substantially more than $144 million." These proceeds would be contributed to the borrower group to enhance credit. Schaeffer characterized this as "a nice to have, but not a must-have."

On timing, Schaeffer noted a make-whole provision that would cost approximately $13 million if the 2027 notes are repaid before mid-June, suggesting Cogent could structure a transaction to close into escrow and fund repayment once the make-whole declines to zero.

The refinancing plan is executable in a normal credit environment. The risk is that markets tighten precisely when Cogent needs access, or that the data center sale collapses again (a prior two-facility LOI fell through after last-minute renegotiation). Investors face real rate and market-condition risk even as management prepares a like-for-like secured refinancing.

## Margin Trajectory: The Genuinely Good News

Beneath the revenue noise, Cogent's margin story is improving meaningfully—often overlooked because reported EBITDA is distorted by the mechanical step-down in T-Mobile IP transit payments.

Full-year adjusted EBITDA margin fell to 30.0% from 33.6%, but that decline was entirely a function of $104.2 million in lower T-Mobile IP transit payments and $21.4 million in reduced reimbursable Sprint acquisition costs—both contractually predetermined. On an underlying basis, EBITDA margins expanded by roughly 800 basis points year-over-year, driven by cost-cutting and the shift toward high-margin on-net services.

On-net revenues have risen to 61% of total revenue, up from 47% in Q3 2023. This is the structural improvement management has been engineering since the Sprint deal closed: shedding low-margin off-net contracts and replacing them with high-margin on-net and wavelength connections.

Schaeffer has outlined multi-year objectives of 6%–8% annual top-line growth, approximately 200 basis points of annual margin expansion on average, and maintaining a debt maturity profile that provides adequate runway and liquidity.

The math, if it works, is compelling. If Cogent exits 2027 with revenue around $1.1 billion and EBITDA margins in the 33%–35% range, it generates roughly $360–385 million of adjusted EBITDA. Against net debt of approximately $2.5–2.7 billion, leverage would be 6.5–7.5x—still elevated, but with a visible path toward 4x as T-Mobile payments wind down and organic growth compounds.

## Valuation: Cheap, But Not Obviously Safe

At roughly $18 per share, Cogent's market capitalization is approximately $860–900 million. Add $2.9 billion in total debt, subtract $205 million in cash (including $56.6 million restricted), and enterprise value is approximately $3.6 billion. Against consensus 2026 EBITDA of roughly $413 million, that implies an EV/EBITDA multiple of about 8.7x.

That is inexpensive for a Tier 1 internet backbone operator with 57-country reach, 1,902 data center access points, and a rapidly growing optical wavelength business. Analyst price targets range from $21 to $52, reflecting the wide dispersion of views. Goldman Sachs recently cut its target to $22 from $25, maintaining a Neutral rating and citing revenue misses despite in-line EBITDA. UBS lowered its target to $21 from $27, pointing to more moderate wavelength growth.

The price-to-sales ratio at current levels is approximately 0.9x—well below historical norms for a business with this asset quality and market position. But "cheap" on a static multiple means little if the refinancing stumbles or the wavelength ramp disappoints. The stock reflects genuine uncertainty, not irrational pessimism.

## Key Risks: Where the Thesis Breaks

**Refinancing failure or adverse terms.** The $750 million unsecured note refinancing is the most pressing risk. If credit markets seize or lenders demand significantly higher rates, the interest burden becomes unmanageable. At 7% on $750 million, annual debt service is $52.5 million; a meaningful step-up would further constrain cash generation.

**Wavelength ramp disappoints.** At $38.5 million in 2025 revenue, wavelength is still embryonic. If the ramp slows—due to competitive pricing from incumbents, slower data center connectivity buildout, or Cogent's inability to penetrate its target market—the growth narrative collapses and total revenue continues declining.

**Executive-level uncertainty.** In August 2025, reports emerged that lenders had seized a portion of CEO Schaeffer's pledged shares following personal margin calls. While this does not directly impact company operations, it introduced a layer of uncertainty that has weighed on sentiment. Schaeffer is the architect of this company and deeply embedded in its culture and strategy. Any management disruption would be disproportionately damaging.

**IPv4 market deterioration.** The IPv4 intangible is carried at $458 million and is not amortized. A significant decline in lease rates or market liquidity could trigger an impairment review and force a material write-down, hitting book value and potentially raising debt covenant scrutiny.

**Restricted cash mechanics.** The IPv4 securitization structure includes covenants tied to leverage and debt-service-coverage ratios. Restricted cash of $56.6 million becomes available based on improvements in those metrics. If performance falls short, this represents a modest but real liquidity constraint.

## The Investment Case in Plain English

Cogent is a Tier 1 internet backbone provider that acquired the Sprint wireline network from T-Mobile for a purchase price of $1 and received $700 million in contractual T-Mobile payments to manage the transition. That transition is now nearly complete: the bad revenue is almost gone, and the good revenue—on-net internet, wavelength, IPv4 leasing—is growing.

The stock trades near $18 because the dividend was effectively eliminated, revenue declined in 2025, and a $750 million debt maturity is approaching. Those are all real problems.

But the core assets—a nationally contiguous, owned fiber network; Tier 1 peering status connecting 7,659 networks; 1,902 data center access points; 38 million IPv4 addresses; and 1,068 wavelength-enabled locations—are not impaired. If anything, the network is more valuable today than three years ago, precisely because demand for optical transport from AI and cloud infrastructure is accelerating.

The investment thesis is not "Cogent is fine." It is: "Cogent's genuine assets are worth substantially more than $18 per share, the refinancing is executable, and the wavelength ramp can offset Sprint revenue decay within 12–18 months." That thesis requires several things to go right simultaneously: a functioning high-yield market, sustained wavelength contract wins, and disciplined cost management.

For investors who can stomach the binary nature of the near-term risk—particularly around the refinancing—CCOI below $20 offers asymmetric upside if the thesis plays out. Schaeffer has reiterated Cogent's goal to return to 4x net leverage, which he said would position the company to resume a return-of-capital program similar to the one it operated for 15 years. Even a modest dividend or buyback program, against a sub-$1 billion market cap, would be a significant re-rating catalyst.

This is not a buy-and-forget investment. It is a leveraged bet on execution—on fiber, on wavelength, and on a CEO who has built this network from scratch and has the most to lose if it fails. Position size accordingly.
